INTRODUCTION: UNFRAMED

The easiest thing in the world is to be a Noun. The System demands a label: Director, Expert, Heir, Husband. It is the path of least resistance. I just wanted to be a Verb. To be happy. To be myself.

Let's be clear: I won the lottery. I was born at the right time and in the right place, surrounded by a family that provided a safety net of unconditional love. I could afford to fail because I knew they would catch me. This privilege gave me the luxury of the Drift. My career was never a ladder; it was a series of accidents. I didn't chase success; I often just walked down the street and stumbled into it.

Like any memoir, this text flatters its author. In these pages, I am the protagonist of my own myth. Much of my lifestyle was inherited rather than conquered. But this is still my life. For my actions--and for my inaction--I bear the sole responsibility.

I am spoiled, capricious, and narcissistic. I have been arrogant since kindergarten. I make no apologies for this. My achievement is not that I became "good." It is that I remained real.

You will not find the dirty laundry of my private life here. If I publish it, it ceases to be private. I do not trade in the secrets of those who shared my journey. They influenced the code, but they stay off-screen. At times, this narrative may read like a chaotic CV. But jumping from project to project, from industry to industry--this is not a bug; it is a feature of my existence.

I don't blame the weather for my problems, but I didn't live in a vacuum. The Empire collapsed, borders closed, and the world tried to edit my plans for happiness. Yet, through the noise, I kept moving toward my own coordinates.

Yes, I have a "heavy" character. I am a snob. But let's refine the definition. To me, snobbery is not about humiliation; it is about compatibility. It is the desire to remain among equals.

This is a safety protocol. Inequality creates friction. When you mix disparate social layers at one table, you don't get diversity; you get a brawl. Different codes, different languages, different values. It is structurally unsafe. My arrogance is actually a conflict-resolution mechanism. By filtering my circle, I eliminate the noise.

There are two footnotes to this confession. First, I do not weaponize my standards. This high bar is set for me, not for you. I live this way for my own internal hygiene. I never impose my ideas on anyone--unless, of course, they order a consultation and pay the invoice. I am not a missionary; I am a luxury service provider. If you don't pay, I don't preach.

Second, this toxicity evaporates when you are in the right container. When you find yourself in a room of equals--among other difficult, complex, high-functioning people--the pretension is neutralized. A peer will treat you with tolerance. They won't judge your sharp edges because they know exactly how heavy that armor is to carry. As the saying goes: "Render unto Caesar the things that are Caesar's." I stay in my lane, and I expect you to stay in yours. It saves time and preserves the nervous system.

The gurus say: "Find yourself, and everything becomes simple." Nonsense. It took me forty years to excavate myself from the rubble of expectations. It is not a hack; it is a siege.

The world wants to put you in a box. It wants to format you. Even now, as an artist--where every canvas technically demands a boundary--I refuse the constraint.

I stepped out of the stone. I stepped out of the screen. I remain, strictly, Unframed.

PROLOGUE: THE LOOP

I stepped out of my apartment on Kutuzovsky Prospect, the historic habitat of the Soviet elite. It took less than a minute.

Three officers from Center "E"--the state's "anti-extremism" unit--were waiting for me. They didn't look like heroes. They looked like the end of an era. There was no drama. Just the dull bureaucracy of repression.

"Anton Pavlovich?" I nodded. "Let's go."

The loop closed. The granite of the Empire had finally come to collect its debt from the digital ghost. But stop. Let's rewind.

I have a vivid memory, sharp and clear. I am four years old. I am lying on the warm parquet floor. The apartment is usually noisy--phones ringing, adults arguing about high culture or low salaries, the constant hum of the Soviet intelligentsia trying to survive. But in this moment, there is silence. I am drawing.

The parquet was warm. The sun came through the tall windows--those two-meter Stalinist windows designed to make you feel small and the State feel eternal. But I didn't feel small. I felt infinite. The pencil moved across the paper, and the world appeared. Not the world given to me by adults, by teachers, or by the System. The world as I decided it should be.

My mother walked past and smiled. She didn't interrupt. She didn't correct my lines or tell me what to draw. She just smiled and kept walking. My grandfather, a Colonel of the Interior Ministry, didn't scold me for blocking the hallway. He stepped over me carefully, as if I were something precious that shouldn't be disturbed.

For a brief moment, the chaotic energy of our family aligned around a boy with a pencil. I was an Artist. I was happy. And everyone was happy. Of course they were. In a family like ours--surrounded by the legacy of Sergey Merkurov, the sculptor who carved Lenin's face, where culture was oxygen--a child drawing on the floor wasn't a nuisance. It was a validation. It meant the bloodline was intact. It meant the talent was still alive.

The apartment had four rooms. Every wall was lined with books, floor to ceiling. The icons my grandfather collected stared down from their gold frames, silent witnesses to the intellectual chaos below. But the floor was mine. The parquet was my canvas before I had a canvas. It was the one horizontal surface in a vertical world of shelves, and I claimed it.

I don't know why that memory survived when so many others faded. Maybe because it was the last time I felt completely aligned with myself. No analysis. No doubt. Just the hand, the pencil, and the line.

But then, I stood up. I went to school. And I forgot who I was for forty years. I had to grow up. I had to learn how to think, how to survive, how to build networks on frozen rooftops and navigate the chaos of a collapsing empire. I had to become the Expert, the Analyst, the Manager. I had to forget the floor.

The pencil was replaced by the keyboard. The canvas was replaced by the screen. And the warm parquet floor became just another piece of real estate in an apartment that was too expensive to heat. But the memory didn't die. It just waited. Hidden. Quiet. Waiting for the moment when the noise finally stops, and you remember what silence sounds like.











# PART I

CHAPTER 01: THE GRANITE AND THE GLITCH (1980s - 1999)

I was born in the long shadow of the Kremlin. My childhood was spent on the Berezhkovskaya Embankment, inside a "Stalinka"--a monumental apartment building designed for the victors of history. High ceilings, three meters of air above your head, and walls thick enough to survive a bombardment. It was architecture as an assertion of power. My family was the definition of the Soviet elite, but of a specific breed. We worked with our minds, not our hands. My grandfather was a Colonel in the Ministry of Internal Affairs--a man of the system, disciplined and rigid. My mother belonged to a different world entirely; she worked at the Bolshoi Theater. She is still there, a living bridge between the imperial past and the uncertain present, serving the high culture that outlasts every regime. I was never interested in politics; I lived inside it.

My first real friend among the adults was Grigory Morozov. He was a close friend and colleague of my grandfather, a fixture at our table. He also happened to be the first husband of Svetlana Alliluyeva--Joseph Stalin's daughter. I knew the history, of course, but it didn't register as momentous. It was just part of the furniture. The scenery of my childhood.

But the true gravity of the family came from my great-grandfather, Sergey Merkurov. He was "The Granite." The sculptor who immortalized the Empire. He carved the nervous system of the USSR. He erected monuments to Lenin that dominated the squares of half the country. He cast the death masks of the titans: Leo Tolstoy, the chaotic pacifist; Vladimir Mayakovsky, the futurist screamer; Felix Dzerzhinsky, the sword of the Revolution. He captured the final breath of an era in plaster. History has a way of compressing a lifetime of labor into a single bullet point. Despite the monuments and the vast catalog of faces, he is remembered for one thing only. He had taken the death mask of Vladimir Lenin. He touched the face of the Revolution while it was still warm. To the world, this was sacred history.

There is a rare artifact from my school years--a memory that now reads like either a joke or a prophecy that defined the rest of the file.

First grade. Day one. The first homework assignment.

The specific subject is lost to the archives. I remember only the instruction. A single word: "Think."

I returned home and solemnly announced to the family that I was proceeding to execute the task. I retreated to my room.

For twenty minutes, I sat in absolute silence and just... processed. What exactly was I thinking about? Naturally, the data is lost. But the mechanism was active.

I returned to the living room and reported: "Done. I have finished."

I suspect that I haven't actually stopped thinking since that moment. The background process launched that afternoon is still running.

I only mentioned this at school after Ogonyok--a magazine that at the time was essentially a Russian copy of Bild--published a feature on it. Before that, to me, it was just family trivia. This dissonance exploded when I was seven years old. We were being inducted as Octyabryata--the lowest cell of the Communist youth organization. The ceremony took place in the Lenin Museum on Red Square. It was a temple of ideology: hushed tones, dramatic lighting, and the heavy smell of reverence. After the formal vows of loyalty were spoken, we went on an excursion. We stopped near the sanctum sanctorum--the original death mask of Vladimir Lenin. The room was silent. The children stared in awe. "Oh," I said casually, breaking the silence. "My great-grandfather made that." The tour guide turned on me instantly. Her face didn't just show anger; it showed a shock. I had violated the script. I had introduced a personal, human element into their sacred mythology. "You liar!" she snapped, her voice echoing off the marble. "Don't lie, boy." The class froze. The induction was ruined. But then, the unexpected happened. My classmates started shouting, breaking ranks: "He's telling the truth! It's true!" I just laughed. It was a minor episode, a childhood skirmish. But it taught me a vital lesson that would define the rest of my life: The System does not care about the truth. The System hates it when your personal reality contradicts its official mythology.

My family dynamic was defined not by shared belief, but by mutual respect. There was no direct "upbringing" or forced education in the traditional sense. It seemed pointless from an early age; I developed my own opinions and rarely compromised them. I was a difficult child--capricious, loud, demanding. In a standard Soviet family, I would have been broken. I would have been told to sit down, shut up, and behave. But here, I was respected. My personhood was treated as a sovereign state. No matter how much noise I made, the safety net of absolute, unconditional love remained intact. They were charging a battery inside me--filling me with enough confidence to last a lifetime. When I voiced some nonsense--and I voiced plenty--the response was consistent and polite: "Check the books. Read up on the topic." I would return, equally politely, armed with citations, and justify my position. This constant, low-intensity intellectual sparring prepared me for the world better than any school curriculum ever could.

CHAPTER 02: THE LIBRARY AND THE LIE

The apartment was vast, over 100 square meters--a luxury of space that was almost indecent by Soviet standards. But the real geography of my childhood wasn't measured in square footage. It was measured in volumes.

My grandfather, a Colonel of the Interior Ministry, had a peculiar habit for a man of his rank. He was a Jew who collected Orthodox icons. In any other context, this would be a contradiction. In ours, it was just texture. The gold-leafed, dark faces of saints looked down on us from the walls, sharing space with paintings and artifacts. For me, these icons were simply part of the ordinary stage design. I felt no specific holiness toward them, and I understood that my grandfather, a man of the rigid system, viewed them in the same pragmatic light--as salvaged objects of art and culture. There was no internal conflict, only the complex reality of intellectual survival in a country that had just finished destroying churches.

The true legacy of the family wasn't found in the plaster or marble scattered on the floor. It was in the paper. After his retirement, my grandfather devoted himself to the archive of his father, Sergey Merkurov. He was compiling the history of the family. I understood perfectly well why he did this: it was his duty to his father's immense body of work. I watched him meticulously sorting papers, folding letters, organizing countless newspaper clippings, photos, and slides into massive folders. The apartment smelled of old glue and cigarette smoke. This work eventually led to the publication of an album featuring S.D. Merkurov's works. As a child, I showed no interest. The dead sculptor was just furniture in my head. But my grandfather didn't care. He was preserving the legacy while the Soviet Union crumbled outside.

But the real action happened on the shelves. Walls were lined with encyclopedias. This library became the graveyard of my childish beliefs and the birthplace of my lifelong cynicism. I had access to two distinct versions of reality, sitting side by side:

On the left: *Brockhaus and Efron*, the pre-revolutionary encyclopedia. Thick, leather-bound volumes printed before 1917. The world of the Tsar, gold, aesthetics, and God.  
On the right: *The Great Soviet Encyclopedia*. Red spines. The world of the Party, concrete, quotas, and Dialectical Materialism.

I devised a game. I would open them to the same letter and compare the definitions. It was my first experience with how history is edited in real time.

Take the word "Merchant" (*Kupets*).  
In *Brockhaus*, a merchant was a respected pillar of society, a philanthropist, a driver of commerce and civilization. The text praised his contributions to art, education, and public welfare. The merchants built theaters. They funded universities. They were the engine of progress.  
In the *Soviet Encyclopedia*, the definition shifted violently. He was a parasite, a class enemy exploiting the labor of the working class. A bloodsucker who extracted value from the proletariat. A criminal element destroyed by the Revolution.

You have to understand: in the Soviet Union, the algorithm of "buy low, sell high" wasn't called Business. It was called Speculation--a criminal offense punishable by prison or a bullet. Same word. Two different truths. Two different moralities.

I tried other words. "Church." "Property." "Freedom." Every entry was a case study in how language is twisted by power. The *Brockhaus* version described the world as it was. The Soviet version described the world as it should be, according to the Central Committee.

Naturally, I decided to audit God. I didn't look at the sky; I opened the Encyclopedia to the letter "G". I cross-referenced the detailed articles from both editions. I read about Zeus, Ra, and Yahweh, and realized they follow the same template: an omnipotent authority figure, a moral code enforced by fear, and a promise of afterlife as compensation for obedience.  
The conclusion was clear: religion was a construct, an invention of humanity. The sociological function was obvious--it is a control system. It works because it is unfalsifiable. You can't disprove Heaven, so the priests always win the argument.

This immunity to evidence disgusted me. I preferred mathematics. 2+2=4 in every jurisdiction.  
People say: "But without God, where do you get morality?" My answer: the same place you get taste. You don't need a holy book to know that killing is bad or that Beethoven is better than a car alarm. You just know, if your circuitry isn't damaged.

Losing God didn't make me a nihilist. It made me a materialist. I believe in what I can touch, measure, and verify. Love? Yes--it has measurable effects on cortisol and oxytocin. Beauty? Yes--it triggers consistent patterns in the brain. The Motherland? No--it's a legal fiction enforced by men with guns.

This wasn't just a lesson in theology; it was a lesson in sovereignty. If the books lie, the teachers lie, and the State lies, where is the baseline? I realized then that I couldn't outsource my understanding of the world. I had to become the zero-point.  
From that moment on, I stopped looking for 'Objective Truth' out there. I became the Objective Truth. My perception is the only verified asset I own. It's not arrogance; it's calibration. I don't trust the signal until I process it. My opinion isn't just a view; for me, it is the only reality that actually exists.

My great-grandfather carved history into granite to make it look permanent. He erected monuments to Lenin in dozens of cities, each one weighing tons, built to last a thousand years. He tried to freeze time in stone. I learned from the books that history is a fluid. It takes the shape of whatever container holds it--or whoever holds the gun.  
This wasn't philosophy. This was a survival skill. I was seven years old, sitting on the parquet floor, surrounded by the intellectual debris of two dead empires, learning the most important lesson of my life: how to read between the lines, how to detect the lie inside the truth, and how to never, ever trust a single source.

The apartment was vast. But my world was even larger. It contained multitudes, contradictions, and competing realities. I didn't know it yet, but I was being trained to live in the only country that actually exists: the one where everything is simultaneously true and false, depending on who's asking and what year it is.

CHAPTER 03: THE CANON AND THE GLITCH

Of course, they tried to induct me into the world of art. Or at least, they made the attempt. My grandmother took me to Leningrad (now Saint Petersburg), dragging me through the Hermitage and the Russian Museum.  
For some reason, I always liked Karl Bryullov's *The Last Day of Pompeii*. Perhaps, even then, a sense of taste was being unconsciously calibrated--surrounded by paintings at home, and paintings in museums.  
But out of sheer protest, I denied it all.

I remember when I was brought to the Pushkin Museum of Fine Arts--an institution my great-grandfather had actually directed in the 1950s. After the excursion, the legendary director of the museum, Irina Antonova, approached me. She looked down at me and asked:  
"Well, tell me, what did you like the most?"  
"The pies in the buffet," I answered honestly.

This attitude defined my early intellectual life. I have a confession that usually horrifies the Russian intelligentsia: I ignored the Canon. I didn't open *War and Peace* until I was 35. I had no interest in the emotional turbulence of fictional characters; their suffering seemed inefficient.  
The only exception was children's literature, provided it was well-illustrated. I loved *The Wizard of the Emerald City*--the Russian version of the Oz stories--along with *Karlsson-on-the-Roof* and the *Moomins*. I wasn't looking for drama; I was looking for worlds with clear rules and vivid imagery.

My real addiction was structure. The library was arranged by height, and my access to information was dictated by my physical growth. To know more, I literally had to grow taller. I pulled volumes like pulling slot machine levers, looking for the jackpot of a fact.  
This is where I learned the most important skill of my life: Information Arbitrage.  
At the time, none of this was conscious. I wasn't trying to build a career or a mindset; I was just playing a game with the books on the shelves. The realization that this process was actually my training as an analyst only came many years later.

There is an irony in my bloodline regarding this reliance on hard data. Another relative of mine was George Gurdjieff, the famous mystic and philosopher. Yet, despite this genetic link to the esoteric, I grew up despising magic, astrology, and all forms of proofless mysticism. My great-grandmother might have been a specialist in Ancient Egyptian mythology, and my blood might have carried the genes of a mystic, but the Encyclopedias had already done their work.  
I didn't become a believer. I became a knower.  
This defined my entire life and career. I refused to trust the map; I had to walk the territory.

CHAPTER 04: THE SMELL OF ABROAD

The Empire didn't die in silence; it died in a queue. While the country stood in line for "blue chickens"--scrawny, deficit birds that looked like they had died of depression rather than slaughter--our fridge was a diplomatic sovereign state. From time to time, we received "The Zakaz" (The Order). Brown paper packages delivered directly from the Ministry. Sturgeon, buckwheat, candies that didn't exist in the wild. I learned the First Rule of Soviet Life early: Resources are not earned. They are distributed based on your physical distance from the Kremlin wall.

But my bubble wasn't hermetic. The house on Berezhkovskaya was a vertical slice of the Soviet caste system. Yes, there were the apartments of the elite. But there was also the "Limita"--the quota workers brought into Moscow to do the jobs Muscovites refused to do. In the crowd, the grandson of "The Granite" looked exactly like the son of a factory worker. I wore the same cheap Chinese puffer jacket--formless, synthetic insulation against the Russian winter.

To understand the dissonance in my head, however, you have to understand the smell. While the streets smelled of cordite, exhaust, and unwashed bodies, there were pockets of a different air. Sheremetyevo-2 Airport. Stockmann Department Store. Pizza Hut. These weren't just commercial venues; they were embassies of a different reality. I remember the smell vividly. It is hard to explain to someone who hasn't lived behind the Iron Curtain, but "Abroad" has a specific chemical composition. It's in the laundry detergent, the glossy paper, the air conditioning, the plastic wrapping. Take a can of Coca-Cola. Open a Russian can (or a Polish fake from a kiosk). It smells like Russia--sweet, flat, slightly desperate. The bubbles die quickly. Now, open a can brought from Europe. It smells like aluminum, aggressive carbonation, and quality control. It smells like Civilization. We didn't care about "Freedom"--that was an abstract word for the politicians on TV. We cared about "Civilization." We didn't want the right to vote; we wanted the world where the packaging didn't tear, the zippers didn't jam, and the soda didn't taste like betrayal.

To understand the dissonance in my head, you have to look at my summers. They weren't spent in resorts. They were spent in Tarusa. Tarusa is a mythic place. A sanctuary for the dissident intelligentsia on the banks of the Oka River. The ghosts of Sviatoslav Richter and Marina Tsvetaeva walk these streets. It is the Russian equivalent of the Hamptons for the mind, not the wallet.

But I wasn't there just to recite poetry or admire the landscape. I was there for my summer practice at the local stables. I spent my days with the horses--grooming, feeding, riding. It was physical, grounding work. It taught me early that if you want to control a powerful animal, you have to respect it first.

My social life was a schizophrenia of extremes. One evening, I would be sitting in the ancestral home of Vasily Vatagin, the legendary animalist sculptor. We played board games with his great-grandchildren, surrounded by history, art, and the polite quiet of the old aristocracy. The next evening, I was in the local cemetery. We drank samogon (moonshine) sitting directly on the grave of Konstantin Paustovsky, the great Russian writer.

It wasn't blasphemy; it was just the texture of life. On one side, the high-minded dacha dwellers discussed art, philosophy, and the fate of Russia. On the other, the local village youth drank industrial alcohol and fought at the disco until blood was drawn. I belonged to both. I was the perfect image of the "Russian Intelligentsia" we lost: high culture standing knee-deep in mud, trying to keep its balance, with a volume of poetry in one hand and a bottle of moonshine in the other.

Violence wasn't an event; it was the ambient soundtrack of the city. It is a surreal experience to watch a revolution on CNN and then hear the tank fire outside your own window. The Russian "White House"--the seat of the government--was clearly visible from the roof of my building. I didn't need a TV to see the smoke; I just needed to look up.

1991, 1993--the dates blur in my memory, but the audio remains distinct. We would be sitting in class, drawing or passing notes. Somewhere outside, the heavy thump of a grenade launcher would hit a nearby building. The glass would vibrate. We barely looked up. If the weather is rainy, you take an umbrella. If the weather is "artillery fire," you stay away from the window and continue with your algebra.

The city had transformed into a shooting gallery. Politicians, bankers, TV stars--they were all moving targets. The rules were simple: if you were visible, you were vulnerable. If you had money, you had enemies. If you had power, you had a timer ticking over your head.

I remember walking near the Kievsky Railway Station, by the Radisson Slavyanskaya Hotel. In those years, the Radisson was the fortress of the new Western presence--a hub of American business in a sea of post-Soviet chaos. I watched it being built. The construction workers were the first distributors of the new world. We, the local kids, would hang around the fence, and they would toss us the artifacts of the West: chewing gum with frantic colors and beer in aluminum cans. The gum was stupidly sweet. The cans were impossibly thin. Everything about them screamed "imported." We didn't have that kind of aluminum. We didn't have that kind of sugar.

Years later, I saw Paul Tatum, the American hotelier of that same Radisson, gunned down. Right there, near the entrance. In a normal city, witnessing a contract killing is a trauma that defines a decade. You would need therapy. You would have nightmares. In 90s Moscow, it was just Tuesday. We didn't cry. We didn't run. We gossiped. "Did you see? Yes, right by the entrance. Professional job."

We lived in a split reality. There was the street: dirty, smelling of cheap tobacco, unwashed bodies, and desperation. And then there was the bubble. If you had the right paper in your pocket--US Dollars or Deutsche Marks--Moscow was a paradise.

Pizza Hut on Kutuzovsky Prospect was the perfect architectural metaphor for the country. It literally had two entrances. The first door was for the ruble crowd: a simple takeaway counter where people stood in long, gloomy queues to buy a slice of exotic dough with rapidly devaluing paper money. The line moved slowly. The faces were tired. The pizza was a luxury they could barely afford, but they stood there anyway, because it was American, and America was winning. The second door was for the currency crowd: a restaurant with table service, air conditioning, and imported smiles. Real chairs. Real menus. Real napkins. It was an airlock between two dimensions.

Outside, the granite of the Empire was crumbling into dust. Inside, the air smelled of oregano and civilization. Thanks to my background, I learned early which door to open. I realized that safety, comfort, and dignity were not rights provided by the State. They were subscription services, payable in hard currency.

The deficit was the great equalizer. Even with dollars in my pocket, I was still a Soviet schoolboy. Walking home from school, my eyes scanned the street like a radar. If I saw a queue forming--that unmistakable, dark snake of human bodies--I didn't ask what they were selling. I simply joined the tail. I would hold the spot. Then I would run home, alert my family, and they would relieve me to complete the transaction. Usually, it was for cigarettes or basic food. Sometimes it was for something stranger--imported shampoo, canned meat, light bulbs. The content didn't matter. The queue was the signal. If people were standing in line, it meant something scarce had appeared, and scarcity was currency.

Inside our apartment, we had our own portal to the West. The VCR. It appeared early, a black monolith of entertainment smuggled in from abroad. We had the standard diet of the time: action movies with nasal, monotone translation done by a single bored voice-over artist who read every character in the same flat tone. And, of course, Caligula. My first exposure to pornography was high-budget imperial decadence. Malcolm McDowell in a toga, surrounded by naked Romans, and a single Russian voice explaining the plot without emotion.

For those who didn't have a VCR, there were the "Video Salons"--small, smoke-filled rooms where people paid rubles to watch a small TV screen in collective silence. Twenty people crammed into a basement, staring at Die Hard or Basic Instinct, breathing the same stale air. I never saw the appeal. Why sit in a basement when you can consume the signal at home? The VCR was my first taste of on-demand reality. I could pause the West. I could rewind it. I could watch the same explosion three times in a row. The television was controlled by the State. The VCR was mine.

CHAPTER 05: THE BASEMENT

School became irrelevant. I didn't want to sit in a classroom; I wanted to be underground. Moscow in the 90s was built on a honeycomb of unlocked cellars. These weren't the clean storage spaces of today. They were dark, warm, autonomous zones that smelled of damp concrete, rust, and cat urine. This was our club. This was our womb.  
We made money the way all teenagers did in the 90s: however we could. We washed cars, hauling buckets of water from dark basements or dipping them straight into the Moscow River. We collected glass bottles for the deposit--in those years, this wasn't yet a marker of homelessness; it was just valid currency. We unloaded trucks at markets.  
I remember spending my first real earnings from one of those unloading shifts. I didn't buy food or clothes. I went to a beauty salon and paid to have my ear pierced with a "gun." It wasn't regulated yet; you just paid and they shot the stud through your lobe. None of this was "business." It was just life. Everyone lived like that.

Outside, the city was a shooting gallery. When you can see the smoke from the White House from your roof and hear grenade launchers during algebra class, the curriculum loses its authority. I started smoking at twelve and drinking at fourteen. It wasn't a social ritual; it was anesthesia. We drank cheap vodka--Rasputin, with the winking mystic on the label, or Gzhelka. There was a grim irony in the latter: named after delicate blue-and-white Russian ceramics, it tasted like industrial solvent. We drank it warm, straight from the bottle, with no food to chase it down. One bottle, and the lights went out.

We went to metal concerts, seeking noise to drown out the silence. The venues were always the same: half-legal clubs in industrial zones, concrete boxes with no ventilation and sound systems held together with duct tape. Violence was constant. It was the friction between subcultures--the metalheads versus the Gopniks, the tracksuit warriors who hated anyone who looked different.  
I remember one night vividly. A fight broke out near the stage. A knife flashed under the strobe lights. A guy fell, and blood pooled on the dirty floor. He died right there, practically in my arms. I didn't cry or panic. I stepped over him and left. I worried about it on the metro ride home--not about his death, but about whether anyone had seen my face clearly. By the time I unlocked my door, I had already archived the event. File closed. In those years, death was just a statistical probability of the night.

Even while destroying my brain, I was analyzing the process. I remember sitting on a crate in the dark, feeling the chemical burn in my throat, and reading a stolen medical textbook on the pathophysiology of alcoholism. I read about ethanol metabolism, liver enzymes, and the progressive destruction of neural pathways. I wasn't drinking to have fun; I was drinking to stop the analytical machine in my head from overheating. I wanted silence.  
This analog loop of self-destruction could have lasted forever. I was on a trajectory to become another casualty of the transition period. My grandfather had died, and my grandmother passed away shortly after. My mother worked constantly at the Bolshoi to keep us afloat. I was left with too much time and too much darkness.

The system eventually rejected me. They kicked me out of school in the 9th grade. The official reason was "inappropriate appearance and poor academic performance," but the truth was simpler: I had pierced my ears and stopped pretending to care. I wore metal band t-shirts. I showed up late. I didn't do homework. The teachers saw a delinquent; I saw a waste of time. The system offered me a choice: reform or leave. I left.  
My next stop was the PTU--a vocational trade school. In Russia, it was a dumping ground for broken kids--the children of alcoholics and orphans. The curriculum was simple: teach them a trade to keep them off the streets. I enrolled in metalworking and learned to be a locksmith.  
The irony wasn't lost on me: the great-grandson of the man who carved Lenin's face was being trained to fix pipes. But I didn't complain. The PTU had one advantage over regular school: nobody cared who you were. There was no ideology. No pretense. Just the work. I was good at it. For the first time in years, I got straight A's. Not because I suddenly respected authority, but because the tasks were concrete. You either cut the metal correctly, or you didn't. You either assembled the mechanism, or it didn't work. There was no room for bullshit. I liked that.  
But after a year, I realized this wasn't mine either. I could be a good locksmith, but I didn't want to be one. The basement was calling, but no longer for the vodka.

I stopped showing up to the PTU. However, I needed a diploma to enter any institute later. So I bought one. In the 90s, everything was for sale. I found a guy who knew a guy, and I paid cash. A week later, I had a piece of paper that said I had completed my education. The paper was real; the education was not. I looked at the embossed seal, the signatures, the bureaucratic gravity of it all--and I felt nothing. No pride, no guilt. Just the cold satisfaction of having solved a logistics problem. The system wanted documents; I gave it the appearance of compliance. The real education was happening somewhere else entirely.

Society is a machine powered by guilt. From childhood, we are fed a script of obligations: 'Finish your plate,' 'Finish the book,' 'Get the diploma,' 'Don't let the team down.' We are taught that endurance is a virtue and quitting is a sin.  
I never accepted this contract. To me, the only valid compass is my own internal state. If a process brings me misery, I terminate it. Why should I sacrifice my actual, finite time for someone else's abstract goal? Why should I suffer to tick a box on a form?  
I don't believe in 'sunk costs.' I believe in cutting losses. If the movie is bad, I walk out of the cinema. If the job is boring, I walk out of the office. I owe nothing to the institution, the collective, or the 'greater good.' I answer only to myself.

CHAPTER 06: THE DIGITAL AWAKENING

Before the American hardware arrived, there was the BK-0010. A Soviet microcomputer connected to a television set. It had a membrane keyboard that felt like dead skin and a monochromatic interface that offered zero entertainment. It was the digital equivalent of a Lada. But it was important. It taught me that a screen isn't just for watching; it's for inputting commands. It prepared the neural pathways for what was coming.

The hardware appeared on my desk like an artifact from an alien civilization. A beige box--an IBM 486DX--and the key to the universe: a US Robotics Sportster modem. 36.6 baud. The screeching, static scream of a digital connection, followed by the hiss of silence, was the most beautiful music I had ever heard. It was the sound of freedom. It was better than any metal band. Better than any bottle.

I didn't stop drinking immediately, but I stopped going to the basement to rot. I started going there to build. The internet didn't exist yet; there was FidoNet. It was decentralized, anarchic, and strictly hierarchical--a network of computers connected by phone lines, passing messages like digital samizdat.  
It was a meritocracy of pure text. To stand out from the gray mass, you had to own the language. If you wrote smartly, you became a Node. If you wrote stupidly, you were disconnected. My social circle exploded. I met people there who would become famous figures, captains of industry, and lifelong friends. We lived in a hybrid reality long before it became a global standard. Part of the time was spent "Online"--glued to the screen, typing furiously. The other part was spent at the "Fidopoyka"--the Fido drinking party.

It was my first massive experience in communication. The core of the network was small--a few hundred active voices against a background of thousands of silent readers. To belong to that Core, you had to prove your worth both verbally and textually. We were an elite without money, defined only by our ability to process information.  
The structure was beautiful in its brutality. At night, when phone rates dropped, the network would wake up. Modems would dial each other. By morning, your messages had traveled across the city, the country, and sometimes the world. No central server. No company. No government. Just people and wires.

Politics usually existed in the background, but in April 1997, it touched the wire. The bureaucrats decided to introduce "Povremenka"--metered billing for local calls. For us--the FidoNet nodes and modem addicts--it was a death sentence. We stepped out of the digital shadows and held my first conscious political rally. It wasn't for democracy or human rights; it was for bandwidth.  
We gathered near the White House with signs that read "Povremenka Must Die." It was a surreal sight: a few hundred long-haired computer geeks standing in the cold, blinking in the sunlight like vampires, demanding that the flow of data remain free. The police looked at us with confusion. We didn't fit any known category of dissident.  
Today, when I hold an ultra-powerful smartphone connected to a high-speed cloud, all of this looks cute, maybe even funny. But back then, that beige box wasn't just a computer. It was a window into the big world. And I had just climbed through it.

We didn't win the tariff battle, but the market solved it for us. Broadband arrived, landlines died, and the concept of "calling a house" became obsolete.  
Then came the real Internet via dial-up. It was slow and expensive. We bought scratch cards in kiosks, rationing our minutes like oxygen. We navigated by the Rambler Top100, and mostly, we went to Krovatka ("The Little Bed").  
Krovatka was a legendary web chat where thousands of people met in total anonymity. It was the first time I realized the power of the avatar. In the chat room, age and muscle didn't matter. The fact that I was a skinny teenager with circles under my eyes didn't matter. Only your words mattered. I spent nights there, honing my rhetoric, typing faster than I could think.

We developed a specific kind of cynicism to filter the weak. I remember a popular joke we played on newcomers. We would type into the main chat: "System Tip: If you unplug your modem wire and plug it back in very quickly, your connection speed will double." We would watch with cruel satisfaction as user after user timed out. *User\_19 has left the chat. Natasha\_77 has left the chat.* It was a literacy test. If you fell for it, you didn't deserve the bandwidth.

But Krovatka was for the masses. The professionals sat in IRC (Internet Relay Chat). I migrated with the former Fido veterans to the channel #fidorus.  
IRC was the first real example of mass real-time communication. ICQ didn't even exist yet. Looking back, it was pure technological delight--a complex network of multiple servers and clients for different systems. And, of course, it offered the ability to talk to friends 24/7. Whenever you logged in, day or night, someone was always there.  
It was then that I understood that geography is a prison. When you are a child, your social horizon is limited to your courtyard. The Web cracked the wall. Suddenly, I wasn't limited to the idiots in my neighborhood. I was connected to people who spoke my language. My room became a command center. My nickname became my real name. My physical body was just a vessel for the mind that lived in the wires.  
The physical world was messy, unfair, and ruled by idiots with guns. The digital world was clean, logical, and ruled by those who wrote the code. I made my choice. I moved into the machine.

CHAPTER 07: ARCHITECTS OF THE GLITCH

Dial-up was a bottleneck. We needed speed, and the market refused to give it to us. The major ISPs laughed at me. "Berezhkovskaya Embankment? No coverage. Impossible." They saw a dead zone. Geographically, they were right: my neighborhood was a narrow strip of land squeezed between the massive Kievsky Railway Station on one side and a thermal power plant on the other. Just five residential buildings trapped in an industrial vice. Too much interference, too few customers. They saw a problem. I saw a captive market. I decided to build my own provider. This was the first phase of my expansion: The Neighborhood ISP.

I gathered a crew. We weren't engineers; we were teenagers with a drill and a total lack of fear. The plan was simple: if the ISPs wouldn't come to us, we would build the infrastructure ourselves. We went up to the roofs. Imagine the Moscow winter. Minus 20 degrees Celsius. The wind from the Moscow River cuts through your jacket like a knife. The roof is covered in ice. Your fingers go numb in minutes, even with gloves. And there we were, balancing on the edge of a seven-story Stalinka, running coaxial cables from building to building. One slip, and you're dead. No safety harness. No insurance. Just gravity and the certainty that if you fell, your parents would find out you'd been running an illegal ISP on the side. But we didn't care about gravity. We cared about ping.

We drilled through walls built to withstand Nazi artillery. The Stalinka construction was absurdly solid--three-meter-high ceilings, brick walls half a meter thick. Our drill bits overheated and broke. We went through dozens of them. The noise was deafening, but the neighbors didn't complain. Most of them were old Soviet intelligentsia who understood that progress requires sacrifice. Plus, we promised them free internet once the network was live. We pulled kilometers of black cable through ventilation shafts and attics. We built a physical network with our bare, frozen hands. We connected the neighborhood. By the time we finished, I had created one of the first private ISPs in the district. My computer wasn't just a screen anymore; it was a server. A Node. The business model was simple: neighbors paid a monthly fee, and we maintained the infrastructure. It wasn't much money, but it was enough to keep upgrading the hardware. More importantly, it gave me something I hadn't had before: legitimacy.

Between the roofs and the boardrooms, the reality cracked again. August 1998. The Financial Crash. I remember the default physically: I developed acute bronchitis. Not from the freezing roofs, but from the tobacco. Overnight, the price of imported cigarettes quadrupled. I switched to the cheapest, nastiest local brand available. My lungs protested immediately. The situation was pure schizophrenia. Those who earned in Rubles were wiped out in a day. Those who held US Dollars--the "currency crowd"--suddenly had their purchasing power multiplied by four. The panic lasted two weeks. Then, the adaptation set in. We got used to the new prices. I cured the bronchitis. And the market cleared itself of the weak, making room for the new species.

By 1999, the skills we learned in the freezing cold and the economic chaos evolved into something resembling a real business structure. This was the second phase: NetArt. With friends from the network--people I had met in the chaos of IRC chats--I launched a system integrator. We weren't just "kids with cables" anymore. We were infrastructure architects for the nascent Big Business of the new Russia. The scope changed completely. We weren't connecting apartments; we were wiring the offices of trading firms, financial structures, and companies that were moving millions of dollars but didn't know how to send an email. These were the winners of the crash--the ones who held the dollars. The aesthetic contrast was sharp. Our clients wore crimson jackets and drove Grand Cherokees. We wore sweaters and arrived on the metro. But the dynamic was clear: They had the capital. We had the connectivity.

NetArt didn't make us rich in the traditional sense. I didn't get stacks of cash. I got something better: free, industrial-grade high-speed internet for years. That was the currency that mattered. My partners were random people from the network--acquaintances who knew how to wire a router. We didn't have formal contracts. We had trust, or at least mutual interest. The money we made went straight back into hardware. Better servers. Faster connections. The cycle fed itself. We designed their local area networks. We procured their servers from dubious importers who brought Compaq and Dell equipment through gray customs channels. We built the digital nervous systems for their primitive capitalism. My business card read "Head of IT Department," even though the "department" was just me and my friends. But the card worked. I learned then that in the corporate world, competence is secondary to confidence. If you can explain to a man who owns an oil brokerage why he needs a Cisco router, and you do it without blinking, he will pay you.

The meetings were surreal. I would sit in their offices--marble floors, leather chairs, secretaries in too much makeup--and explain the basic topology of a local network. They would nod seriously, pretending to understand. I would quote prices that I had made up on the spot, inflating the hardware cost to cover our labor and risk. They would agree without negotiation. Money wasn't real to them. It was just numbers they moved around. But for us, every contract meant new equipment and more freedom.

At the same time, my family pushed me toward formal education. I enrolled in the Institute of Instrument Engineering. It was a concession to their anxieties. They wanted me to have a "real" career, a degree, a safety net. It was excruciating. I sat in lecture halls listening to old men teach from textbooks written before the microprocessor revolution. They were explaining analog circuits while I was building TCP/IP networks. They were teaching DOS while I was running Linux servers. I lasted a couple of months. I walked out one day and never went back. I didn't say goodbye. I didn't submit paperwork. I just stopped showing up. I didn't need a degree to prove I existed. The Network was my university. The modem was my diploma. And the clients who paid me in cash were my grades. The empire of the roofs didn't last forever. By 2000, the major telecom companies had figured out that Moscow was worth investing in. They laid fiber. They built infrastructure that made our coaxial cables look like toys. The market consolidated. But it didn't matter. By then, I had moved on. I had learned the most important lesson of the era: If the system doesn't provide what you need, you build it yourself. First on the roof, then in the office.

CHAPTER 08: THE LIVEJOURNAL ERA

If FidoNet was the digital underground, LiveJournal was the main stage. It wasn't just a blogging platform--it was the forum of a new generation. In early 2000s Russia, LJ was the only place where freedom of speech actually existed. Programmers mixed with poets, future political prisoners drank with future oligarchs. The barrier to entry was intellectual, not financial.

I registered in 2001. My username was "palych"--a casual abbreviation of my patronymic, Anton Pavlovich. I also owned the domain palych.ru, which felt like staking a claim in digital territory. The nickname stuck. For years, people who only knew me online would call me Palych in person, and I never corrected them.

LiveJournal was the opposite of FidoNet in every way. If FidoNet was samizdat passed hand-to-hand in the dark, LJ was a printing press running 24/7 under stadium lights. The key difference was speed. FidoNet updated at night in batches, like a slow heartbeat. LJ was real-time. You wrote something, hit "post," and within seconds people were commenting. It was the difference between a letter and a conversation. Between a bicycle and a car.

I didn't write manifestos. I didn't engage in flame wars. My journal was simply a diary--photos, random thoughts, reports from the latest gathering at Pirogi. As the Russian saying goes: "Chukcha is not a reader, Chukcha is a writer." I consumed content selectively. I read Anton Nossik's blog religiously, but mostly I was there to broadcast, not debate.  
My audience was small but sharp. I didn't have thousands of subscribers--I had the right subscribers. The people who mattered in the tight circle of Moscow's digital elite. In the early 2000s, that was enough. Influence wasn't measured in clicks. It was measured in who showed up when you called a meeting.

The virtual world had a physical headquarters: a café called Pirogi ("Pies") on Bolshaya Dmitrovka Street. It was a smoky, noisy basement that served cheap vodka and expensive conversations. Every Friday, without fail. Sometimes during the week if something urgent needed discussing--a new startup, a political scandal, a technical breakthrough--but Fridays were sacred.  
The seating arrangement defined the era. At neighboring tables, you'd find the future opposition politician Alexei Navalny--young, loud, and not yet a martyr--sitting meters away from the nationalist philosopher Konstantin Krylov. Writers, journalists, coders. The entire spectrum of the Russian intelligentsia compressed into that basement, drinking from the same bottle before history tore them apart.  
The interior was unremarkable. Dark wood tables, low ceilings, air thick with cigarette smoke. The kind of place that would be shut down by health inspectors in any functioning country. But this was Russia in the 2000s. Nobody cared about ventilation. We cared about proximity.

On any given Friday, you might find yourself at a table with Anton Nossik. He deserves a separate mention. By then, Anton was already a living legend--the founder of Lenta.ru, Vice President of Rambler, a prominent Russian-Israeli journalist, and the undisputed king of the blogosphere. Despite the crushing weight of his status, we became friends quickly. Anton possessed a unique gift: he could find a common language with anyone, from oligarchs to coders. He was genuinely kind--a rare trait in our cynical circle--and he helped everyone who asked. But his main asset was his mind. He had a terrifyingly precise memory, deep knowledge of history, and fluency in multiple languages. You could discuss the Torah, Python code, or geopolitics with equal depth. I was twenty-one, ambitious and raw, and incredibly proud to be allowed into his orbit.

The beauty of Pirogi was that--thanks to people like Anton--we didn't talk about anything "important." All the serious discourse happened online. Pirogi was where we stopped performing. We talked about life, about ourselves, about nothing in particular. We ate, drank, laughed. It was the rest between the acts.  
I became famous there for one peculiar habit: I could consume industrial quantities of carpaccio. Raw beef sliced thin, drizzled with olive oil and lemon. Plate after plate. It became a running joke: "Palych is here, order more meat." (I still can, by the way. Some talents don't fade.)

We didn't care about titles. We were young, we were the elite, and we believed the future belonged to us. We thought we were building a new Russia inside the browser window. Oil prices were climbing. The economy was stabilizing. Putin was just a bureaucrat in a gray suit--boring but seemingly competent. The chaos of the 90s was over. The future looked... possible.  
We didn't know yet that the old Russia was waiting outside the door with a pair of handcuffs. But that realization came later. Much later. In the early 2000s, we were still optimistic. Naive, maybe. But optimistic.

Pirogi eventually closed. Other places appeared. The crowd scattered. LiveJournal itself began to change--first bought by an American company, then sold to a Russian oligarch, then slowly suffocated by censorship and irrelevance. The platform didn't die suddenly. It just faded, like an old photograph left in the sun. By the time I realized the era was over, I was already somewhere else. The stage had moved. The audience had moved. The conversation continued, but in different rooms, on different platforms, with a different tone.

The 2000s, looking at the data, were the happiest years for Russia in a century. The economy grew. Salaries doubled, then tripled. You could travel. You could speak. You could build. It wasn't freedom in the Western sense--there were always red lines you couldn't cross--but compared to the 90s, compared to the Soviet years, it felt like a miracle. We didn't see the trap closing. Or maybe we saw it and chose not to believe it. Either way, by the time the handcuffs came out, it was too late to run.  
But in those smoky Friday nights at Pirogi, with vodka on the table and the future wide open, we were invincible. Or so we thought.

CHAPTER 09: THE ACCIDENTAL JOURNALIST

Like everything else in my life, journalism happened by accident. A friend from LiveJournal called me.  
"Want to write about games and computers?"  
"Sure."  
"Come to CDPro."

The name doesn't matter. In those years, the barrier between online acquaintance and real-world collaboration was thin. If you were active on LJ, if you had Reputation, doors opened with a single phone call.

CDPro was a newspaper published by the pirates of the era. This requires context for Western readers. In the early 2000s, before Steam, before digital distribution, Russia ran on pirated CDs. The market was enormous. Street kiosks sold games, software, movies--anything that could be burned onto a disc and wrapped in a photocopied cover. It was a parallel economy, entirely cash-based, entirely illegal, and absolutely essential.  
CDPro served that market. It was a glossy magazine that reviewed games, published walkthroughs, and provided technical support for people trying to install cracked software. The irony was beautiful: a professional editorial operation built on top of mass copyright infringement. But it worked. People bought it. Advertisers paid. And for a brief moment, it was a real newsroom.

The office was somewhere beyond the Garden Ring, near Polezhaevskaya metro station, I think. I don't remember the exact address. It was one of those anonymous Soviet office buildings that had been repurposed for the new economy--gray concrete, fluorescent lights, and the faint smell of old linoleum.  
I learned how to write news. I learned that journalism isn't about inspiration--it's about the deadline. You sit down. You have two hours. You produce 1,000 characters. It doesn't matter if you're tired, hungover, or uninspired. The page needs to be filled. The discipline was useful. It trained me to write fast and clean, without the luxury of endless revision.

I remember two pieces vividly. The first was an article about Linux for home users. I was trying to explain to the CDPro audience--mostly teenagers who wanted to play pirated games--why they should consider switching to an open-source operating system. It was a utopian idea in 2001. It's still a utopian idea now. Linux is powerful, free, and philosophically correct. It's also user-hostile, fragmented, and incompatible with 90% of the software people actually want to use. I wrote the article anyway. I believed in the dream, even if the reality was a nightmare of terminal commands and driver incompatibility.

The second was an interview with Gleb Pavlovsky. Pavlovsky was the political technologist--the "Grey Cardinal" of the Kremlin, the man who engineered the Putin era from the shadows. He was a former Soviet dissident turned regime strategist, a master of information warfare before the term became popular. He was also a LiveJournal user. So in a strange way, we were already acquainted.  
I ran into him at the Non/Fiction book fair. He was just walking around, browsing the stalls, looking relaxed. I approached him with my cheap digital voice recorder.  
"Gleb Olegovich, can I ask you a few questions?"  
He smiled. "Sure."  
The interview was absurd. I didn't ask him about power, about the Kremlin, about the machinery of control. I asked him how he liked the book fair. What he was reading. Small talk. Filler for a pirate gaming magazine. He answered politely, with the patient amusement of a man who knew exactly how irrelevant the conversation was. I was a kid with a recorder. He was the architect of a political system. But he gave me his time anyway.

Years later, we reconnected on Facebook. We chatted occasionally. Shortly before his death, he sent me a message: "Thank you."  
I was confused. "For what?"  
"For everything," he replied.  
I never understood what he meant. Maybe he was thanking everyone in bulk, as people do when they sense the end approaching. Or maybe he remembered something I had forgotten--some conversation, some article, some moment that mattered to him and not to me. I'll never know.

After CDPro, I followed the traditional Russian career path: a period of total idleness, followed by another job found through LiveJournal. (Everything happened through social networks.) I landed at a PR agency located in the iconic Izvestia building on Pushkin Square. The walls still smelled of Soviet ink and old censorship.  
There, I learned the mechanics of information warfare. My task was to aggregate news--taking fragments from different sources and assembling them into a weapon. I watched programmers build a monitoring system that looked suspiciously like a prototype of Google News, years before the West caught up. My specific mission was "Black PR." I was assigned to collect information against Dmitry Gayev, the legendary and controversial head of the Moscow Metro. (He's long dead now, and the Metro is still there.) It was my first step into the world of Viktor Pelevin--a reality where narratives are manufactured in office cubicles by bored young men. It felt like a hallucination. And it certainly wouldn't be the last time I found myself inside one of his novels.

I left eventually. By then, I had learned what I needed to learn: how to meet a deadline, how to structure a story, and how to turn a casual encounter into publishable content. It was my first taste of media power. But it wouldn't be my last.

CHAPTER 10: THE WAR AND THE GREY SUIT

Before the terror came to Moscow, there were two Chechen wars. I wasn't on the front lines, but I understood the signal. It wasn't a "counter-terrorism operation." It was the destruction of independence.

It was supposed to be Ichkeria, not Chechnya. A republic that had declared its sovereignty in 1991, riding the same wave that broke the Soviet Union apart. Chaotic, yes. Violent, yes. But sovereign. The Kremlin decided that this unusual region--Muslim, mountainous, defiant--could not be allowed to exist outside the vertical of power.  
I knew the names: Dzhokhar Dudayev, the former Soviet general who became president of the breakaway republic. Aslan Maskhadov, the moderate who tried to build a functioning state in the ruins. In my eyes, they were not terrorists, not initially. They were normal people trapped in an impossible geometry between warlords, oil pipelines, and imperial reconquest.

I consumed everything. Television news. Newspapers. The early Russian internet. KavkazCenter, the Chechen news site, had just launched. I read their version. I read the Kremlin's version. I cross-referenced, the way I had learned with the encyclopedias as a child. Same war. Two different narratives.

Russia destroyed them. The first war, 1994-1996, was a humiliating defeat for the federal army. Grozny was leveled. Tens of thousands died. Russia withdrew, and for three years, Ichkeria existed in a gray zone--not recognized, but not occupied. Then came 1999. The second war. This time, there would be no retreat.

And in exchange for victory, we got blowback. Salafist-style Islam flooded in, replacing the secular nationalism of Dudayev. We got Beslan--an entire school held hostage, children shot in the back. We got the Moscow theater siege. We got war, terror, and death delivered not as an external threat, but as a bill we had written ourselves. The war was sold as a victory. Flags waved. Medals were awarded. But victory over what? Over a future that could have been different? Over the possibility that a Muslim region could coexist with Moscow on terms other than total submission?

I watched this unfold from my glowing screens in Moscow, refreshing news sites, scanning forums, reading debates in the early LiveJournal threads. And I felt the first cracks in the illusion. The 90s had been chaotic, yes. Violent, yes. But they had been open. You could still argue. You could still leave. You could still imagine alternatives. There were real elections, real debates, real opposition. The future was uncertain, but it was plural. And then, in 1999, the door began to close.

The veterans returned to a city that didn't want to see them. You would see them on the streets--young men with dead eyes, moving through the metro in camouflage jackets, limping on prosthetic legs. Moscow didn't welcome them. There were no parades, no gratitude. Just a collective, uncomfortable silence. We looked away. We crossed to the other side of the platform. The attitude was closer to disgust than respect. They had done the dirty work of the state, and the state discarded them. They became invisible. And we, the comfortable citizens of the capital, were complicit in that invisibility. It was easier not to look. Easier not to ask what they had seen, what they had done.

I didn't know anyone personally who fought or died there. My circle was the intelligentsia, the digital elite, the people who typed rather than pulled triggers. But the war was everywhere, ambient and inescapable, like the smell of smoke from a fire you can't see but know is getting closer.  
The Chechen wars were the rehearsal. The template. The proof of concept. They taught the Kremlin a simple lesson: You can destroy an entire region, kill tens of thousands, erase a city from the map, and the population will not only accept it--they will cheer. All you need is the right narrative. All you need is fear.  
And in 1999, the fear arrived.

Then Vladimir Putin came to power. I didn't like it from the start. What good can come from a former KGB agent? Nothing. It was obvious. The KGB wasn't just an intelligence service like the CIA or MI6. It was the enforcement arm of the Soviet state, designed to detect and destroy anything that didn't follow the rules. You don't retire from that worldview. You carry it in your blood.

When Putin appeared on TV in his gray suit, speaking in that flat, bureaucratic monotone, I saw exactly what he was: a middle manager from the Security Services, promoted beyond his competence. He wasn't a leader. He was an instrument. A figure inserted to execute a specific plan. I was wrong about one thing: I thought he would be temporary. I thought the chaos of the 90s--which was alive, wild, and fierce--would chew him up and spit him out like it had done to everyone else.

I didn't understand yet that chaos is merely an opportunity for control. And the people who put him in power knew exactly what they were doing. There's a popular question in Russia: Who is responsible for this? Who pulled the lever? Who made the call? Who decided that this gray, unremarkable man would be the one to inherit the nuclear codes? I have an answer: Tatyana Dyachenko and Valentin Yumashev. The Family. The inner circle of the dying Tsar, Boris Yeltsin.  
They were not looking for a visionary. They were afraid. They needed someone loyal. Someone who wouldn't prosecute them after Yeltsin stepped down. Someone who would protect the money, the property, the deals. They didn't choose a successor--they chose a guarantee. They chose a lockbox. And the country--exhausted by poverty, humiliated by the loss of empire, longing for stability--accepted the trade. We wanted order. We got a filing cabinet in a suit.

Then came the explosions. Apartment bombings. Buynaksk. Moscow. Volgodonsk. Sleeping families buried under concrete in the middle of the night. The smell of dust and Hexogen. For the majority of the population, these were terrorist attacks blamed on Chechen separatists. The TV said it, so it was true. But to me, the conclusion was immediately clear. The pattern was wrong. The logic was flawed. I looked at the events, and it was obvious: Behind this stood not Chechnya, but the FSB.

The proof arrived in Ryazan. Police discovered a bomb in a basement. The detonator was real. The explosive was Hexogen. The people who planted it were arrested--and they turned out to be FSB agents. The government immediately changed the narrative. "It was just an exercise," they said. "Those were bags of sugar, not explosives."

It was the terrifying question that people were too afraid to ask themselves in 1999: Could the President blow up his own citizens to consolidate power? It was unspeakable. The mind rejects such darkness. It prefers the comfortable lie of external enemies. But now, looking back from 2025--after the systematic murders of political opponents, after the polonium and the Novichok, after the destruction of cities in Ukraine--many people still haven't understood the answer. But I understood it then.  
Yes. He could. And he did.

CHAPTER 11: THE THEATER AND THE PASSPORT

Then came October 2002. Nord-Ost. Chechen terrorists seized a theater in Moscow during a popular musical. 850 hostages. Explosives strapped to women in black veils. The center of the capital turned into a war zone. While the rest of the country was glued to state television, waiting for the curated official updates, we were living it in the browser. This was the first tragedy of the digital age. I sat in front of my monitor, hitting "refresh" on LiveJournal every few seconds. We weren't watching the news; we were aggregating it. Messages from people inside the cordon, rumors from police scanners, posts from relatives.

One of the hostages was a journalist from Interfax agency. She was managing to post updates to LiveJournal directly from inside the theater. For three days, the internet felt like a single, exposed nerve. We believed that publicity would save them. We thought that because the whole world was watching, the government wouldn't dare to do anything reckless. We were wrong.

Then came the "rescue." The government pumped a secret chemical agent--a fentanyl-based opioid gas--into the ventilation system. They knocked everyone out: the terrorists with the detonators and the children in the audience. The special forces stormed the building. They shot the sleeping terrorists. Mission accomplished.

But then the logistics of the "victory" began. They didn't have enough ambulances. They didn't have enough antidotes. They didn't even tell the doctors what gas they had used, so the paramedics didn't know how to treat the victims. I stared at the screen, reading the updates in horror. They weren't evacuating survivors; they were stacking bodies. Unconscious hostages were thrown into buses like firewood, piled on top of each other. Heads lolled back. Airways were blocked. People who had survived the terrorists choked to death on their own vomit in the back of municipal buses.

It was a cold shower for my generation. The encyclopedias had taught me to analyze facts, and the facts were brutal: The State does not care if you survive. The State cares only that the problem is solved. The terrorists were dead. Control was restored. The fact that 130 hostages died in the process was just a statistical error in the report. I realized then that there was no safety here. You are not a citizen; you are collateral damage waiting to happen.

I pulled the emergency brake: my heritage. My great-grandmother--the wife of Sergey Merkurov--was Jewish. I had documents that confirmed this. That was my ticket. I went to the Israeli embassy. I got the papers. I fled.

My first departure wasn't an escape; it was an extraction. Before the plane could take off, I had to survive the bureaucracy. In 2002, the Russian state still treated its citizens like inventory. If you wanted to leave, you weren't just a traveler--you were a defecting asset. To get the stamp "For Permanent Residence Abroad" in my passport, I had to face social workers who looked at me with the eyes of the KGB. "You are betraying your motherland," their silence said. To them, I was property that had decided to walk off the warehouse shelf.

I drank 150 grams of vodka at the airport bar--the standard anesthetic for Russian displacement--and boarded the El Al flight. The ticket was free, paid for by the Jewish Agency (Sokhnut). A transaction: my presence in exchange for their demographics.

It was my first time abroad, and it was a disaster. I landed. I got the paperwork. I got the "Absorption Basket"--the cash handout for new immigrants. I met my LiveJournal friends who had fled earlier. We partied. And then, the silence hit.  
Israel is a miracle in the desert, yes. But to a boy raised in the shadow of the Kremlin, it felt shockingly small. They had a tech industry, sure. But it was strictly backend. An offshore colony writing code for American masters. The money flowed to Silicon Valley; only the wages stayed in Tel Aviv. Can you name a single world-famous Israeli who isn't a politician or a soldier? Besides Netanyahu, the stage was empty.

But the worst part was the Diaspora. I saw what happens to Russians who leave without a plan. They entered a cultural ghetto. They spoke a pidgin dialect--Russian nouns mixed with Hebrew verbs, a linguistic sludge that erased their identity. Their dreams had shrunk. "I have a normal job," they would say with pride. "I am not a cleaner." That was the ceiling of ambition: not being a cleaner.  
I looked at this warm, safe, provincial life, and I felt suffocated. Israel offered safety, but it demanded simplicity. It felt small. Provincial. I am a snob; I admit it. I need the scale of a metropolis. Moscow was dangerous, yes. But Moscow was boiling. It was a supernova of energy, money, and scale. I realized I would rather risk my neck in the Imperial Capital than rot safely in a warm province.

So after one month, I turned around. I didn't care what people thought. My friends in Israel thought I was crazy. My friends in Moscow thought I was reckless. But I knew: safety is boring.  
I flew back to the danger.

The human default is the desire to belong. People crave a container bigger than themselves: a nation, a religion, a football team. I have the opposite instinct. I don't want to join a structure; I want to build one.  
To me, a diaspora is just a province with a better airport. It is a mental ghetto where people trade old stories instead of new ideas. I am interested in the global market, where the only passport is competence.  
Furthermore, it is a matter of pragmatic liability. The moment you identify as 'Jewish' or 'Russian,' you inherit the debts and sins of a history you didn't write. You accept a collective guilt for actions you didn't commit. I refuse the transaction. I want the profit of my own actions, but I will only pay for my own mistakes. I answer only for myself.

It was during this time--between the stamps in the passport, between the departures and returns--that I realized something fundamental about identity.  
Stop calling me a "Russian." This is a classification error. I didn't choose the place of my birth. I didn't choose the language, the climate, or the history. These are just external factors, a series of accidents I had no influence over. I simply happened to be there.

When I landed in Israel in 2002, people asked me: "Do you feel like you have returned home?" I checked my feelings. Did I feel a sudden vibration of the blood? No. Did I feel "Jewish"? No. Did I feel like a citizen of this new state? Absolutely not. I simply had the documents. I saw a situation, I saw the legislation, and I used it. It wasn't a spiritual journey; it was a logistical decision.

This brings me to the strangest phrase in the human vocabulary: "My Country." I find the use of the possessive pronoun here completely absurd. "My painting"--that I understand. I bought the canvas. I picked up the brush. I created it. It belongs to me. If I want, I hang it on the wall. If I want, I sell it. If I want, I take a knife and destroy it. That is property. That is ownership.  
But "My Country"? It is not your property. You do not hold the deed. You cannot sell it, you cannot move it, and you certainly cannot fix it. You are not the owner--you are just a temporary tenant forced to follow rules you didn't write.

And what about the "Motherland"? What does that even mean? Think about the children of military officers who grew up in garrisons, moving to a new concrete town every two years. Where is their root? Where is their soil? I was born in the USSR. If I look for the country of my birth on a map today, it doesn't exist. It vanished. I dislike abstract concepts that can be destroyed by a simple logical question. I don't believe in the "Nation" or the "State." These are fragile ideas that only exist as long as we agree to pretend they are real. I prefer things I can touch, things I can create, and things I can truly own.

Society operates like a predatory bank. It tries to assign you debts you never signed for, the moment you are born.  
You are born a Jew? Here, take the weight of the Holocaust on your shoulders. Carry it. Mourn it. My grandmother survived the Siege of Leningrad. She ate glue to survive. Does that mean I have to perform a ritual of starvation? Does that mean I have to inherit her trauma? No. That was her life. This is mine.  
Even the Granite--my great-grandfather. People assume I carry his legacy out of some heavy, sacred duty. Nonsense. I deal with it because it's interesting. Because it's a high-quality asset. I do it for pleasure and profit. If it were just a burden, I would have walked away years ago.

The world is constantly demanding that you join a group. Pick a trench. Identify with a tragedy. Feel a pain that isn't yours. I refuse.  
It might sound cynical, or "privileged," but let's be honest: I am only truly worried about my problems. And yes, these are often "First World Problems." The temperature of the wine. The quality of the canvas. The delivery date of the new iPhone. The stability of the server connection. These are the things I can touch. These are the things I can control. The rest is just noise trying to guilt-trip me into a subscription I never ordered.

CHAPTER 12: THE LENS AND THE LIMO

Luck in Moscow is not random. I posted a few photos on LiveJournal, and the city responded immediately. A friend introduced me to Vladimir Pavlenko. Vladimir was a real war photographer. He had seen hell through a lens--Chechnya, conflicts, blood. Now he was building a photo agency. The deal was a classic barter: I built his website, and he taught me the trade. I already had the hardware--a digital Sony for speed and a heavy film camera for the soul. But Vladimir gave me something more important than a lens: a laminated piece of plastic that said "PRESS."

My first day on the job was a lesson in the physics of power. I went to Kutuzovsky Prospect--the main artery of the Empire, the direct line from the President's residence to the Kremlin. I witnessed the phenomenon of the "Green Street" (*Zelenaya Ulitsa*). Suddenly, the chaotic, aggressive traffic of Moscow stops. The police clear the road completely. Silence falls in the center of a city of 12 million people. It is a mystical experience. The noise cuts off. Even the birds seem to know the rules and fall silent. Everyone waits for the Czar.

I sat at a bus stop, naive and eager. I decided to snap the perfect shot of the Presidential cortege flying by at 140 km/h. I opened my backpack. I reached for the camera. Before I could even remove the lens cap, they materialized. FSO (Federal Protective Service). The Praetorian Guard. They didn't run; they appeared out of thin air. They checked my bag. The contents were damning: one laptop, one professional digital camera, one analog film camera, one Russian internal passport, one Israeli passport. I didn't look like a photographer. I looked like a parody of a Mossad saboteur sent to assassinate the President, equipped with enough tech to document the act in two formats.

Today, in 2025, this setup would guarantee me a black bag over the head, a basement in Lefortovo Prison, and a 20-year sentence for high treason. But 2003 was different. It was what we called "Vegetarian Times." The regime was still on a diet--it ate grass, not people. We stood there for two hours. They made calls. They checked the database. I explained, I charmed, I flashed the fresh Press Card. And miraculously, reason worked. They realized I wasn't a spy; I was just an idiot with a zoom lens. "Get out of here," the officer said, handing back my passports. I missed the shot. I was late for the editorial meeting. But I walked away free. I learned that day that a Press Card isn't a shield; it's an invitation to a conversation. Back then, the State was still willing to talk.

My photographic career continued, following the same pattern: I fell upwards. I landed at Amedia. The company was founded by Alexander Akopov, a titan of the industry who had just left the position of General Director of VGTRK. He took 15 people--I was one of them--and we began to manufacture the first Russian soap opera, *Poor Nastya*. It was a happy time. I fell out of reality and into the artificial bubble of the Mosfilm soundstages. My world narrowed to actors, producers, and the endless cycle of production. We had location shoots where you would freeze all night and drink that specific, terrible cinema coffee that tastes like burnt plastic.

My role, as usual, was hybrid. I wasn't just a photographer; I was the Gatekeeper. I organized access for other photographers to the set. But the real value was the economics of the skill. Cinema is a specific process, demanding perfect lighting and expensive consumables. Working for Amedia gave me a perfect opportunity: I learned to shoot professionally using Other People's Money. I burned through kilometers of expensive film, experimenting with light and composition, and they paid the bill. Later, I used those skills to shoot the same actresses for glossy magazines. Those photos are still being published today. Technically, for every reprint, I'm supposed to receive a royalty. Honestly, I haven't seen that money in years.

I saw an ad: "Photographer Wanted." It was a startup glossy magazine. The office was on Pushkin Square, in a building with heavy history--it had supposedly housed the office of Andrei Zhdanov, Stalin's ruthless culture ideologue. Decades ago, death sentences for poets were signed here. Now we were building a magazine from scratch, arguing about fonts, choosing paper weight, and debating the color of lipstick.

For a year, I lived the life of a bohemian photographer. My social map shrank to a tight triangle of early "Oil Glamour." My mornings started at Café Moscow-Rome in Stoleshnikov Lane. Years later, this sacred spot would become the legendary Denis Simachev Shop & Bar, the epicenter of the era, but back then it was just a quiet place to cure a hangover. My evenings ended at Café Moscow-Berlin on Tverskaya-Yamskaya. I shot covers. I bought expensive lenses. I played the role of Photo Editor, deciding whose face was worthy of the glossy page. It was a life of free drinks, easy access, and superficial beauty.

A separate vignette from those years perfectly illustrates the mechanics of the city. Moscow was already drowning in gridlock, and public transport was a humiliation I refused to endure. The primary mode of transport was the "hand"--you stood by the curb and negotiated with whoever stopped. But geography, once again, was my ally. My morning commute coincided with the government fleet leaving their garages. Black Mercedes and BMWs with blue flashing lights (*migalki*) cruised past my door on Berezhkovskaya. The drivers were bored, underpaid, and greedy. For 150 rubles--about five dollars--the machinery of the State became my personal taxi service. The trip to the office on Pushkin Square usually took an hour of suffocating traffic. But I had an upgrade option. "Turn on the blues," I would tell the driver. "I'll add extra." We would mount the median strip--the *razdel*, reserved for the gods. Immune to police, immune to the traffic code, we flew toward the center. The trip took ten minutes. It was a scene of pure Moscow absurdity: The Publisher, the serious owner of the magazine, would be parking his civilian Audi A8, exhausted from the gridlock. And right next to him, his twenty-year-old photographer would roll up in a government limousine with the sirens wailing.

On the whole, I reveled in this existence. And why not? I was twenty-one. I had the deliverables: covers, editorials, the full production circus--makeup artists, assistants, studio lights. It felt adult. It felt legitimate. But the real drug was the leverage. Because I was also the Photo Editor, I wasn't just pressing the shutter; I was the Gatekeeper. I had the budget. I could publish other photographers' work and, more importantly, I could sign their checks. In Moscow, the ability to dispense money turns you from a peer into a patron. The level of respect from the community didn't just grow; it verticalized. Even seasoned professionals looked at me differently when they realized I held the keys to the printing press.

But while I was editing retouched faces by day, I was mining deep layers of history by night. To fund a renovation of the family apartment, we decided to monetize the archive. We sold vintage prints from Sergey Merkurov's personal collection--original works by Alexander Rodchenko, Svishchev-Paola, and Moisei Nappelbaum. This was my entry into the real art market. I met the first generation of Russian art dealers. They weren't just buyers; they became partners and friends.

One episode perfectly captures the texture of the time. We organized an exhibition at the Bulgakov Museum--the legendary "Bad Apartment" on Bolshaya Sadovaya, immortalized in *The Master and Margarita*. It was run by the formidable Marietta Chudakova, the country's leading Bulgakov scholar. But this was Moscow. Nothing is simple. The building was a war zone. A rival "museum," run by guys who looked and acted like bandits, occupied the ground floor. They wanted the space. We had to physically hold the line.  
Sometimes this meant guard duty. I remember spending a night locked inside the "Bad Apartment" to prevent a break-in. I slept on a sofa where the literary character Berlioz might have sat before losing his head. I must disappoint the mystics: I felt no supernatural presence. No giant cats, no Woland. I woke up, handed over the shift, and went to shoot another glossy cover. Just business.

Somewhere in the middle of this glossy loop, I actually went to Berlin. Not the café, but the city. It was my second real trip abroad after Israel. I went for a few days with a girlfriend, expecting a heavy, historical city crushed by the weight of the 20th century. Instead, I found a revelation. I remember standing in a park. The scene was chaotic and harmonious. Young people playing football. Young mothers pushing expensive strollers. And right next to them, people openly smoking weed, laughing, lying on the grass. No police. No tension. No aggression. It was absolute, effortless freedom.  
I looked at this tableau and the lingering Soviet question--"Who actually won the Second World War?"--answered itself. We, the victors, were living in a landscape of survival, aggression, and nerves. They, the defeated, were living in a relaxed, socially secure paradise. The "losers" had returned to normal life, where veterans were cared for and the youth were free. The "winners" were still fighting their own ghosts.

I flew back to Moscow with a heavy realization: victory is not a flag on the Reichstag; victory is the quality of life on a Sunday afternoon. I tried to return to the rhythm of the magazine, but the spell was broken. One spring day, I stood on the street outside the office, squinting at the sun. I felt a sudden fatigue. I realized: to be a real photographer, you have to love the process more than the result. You have to love the waiting, the lighting setup, the technical routine. I loved the result--the image, the cover, the impact. But I hated the process. It was becoming a job.  
Aside from the usual personal debris--another failed romance, another burnt bridge--the real reason for the pivot was existential. I faced a binary choice. To remain a photographer meant accepting the service role for the rest of my life. It meant mastering a craft I didn't respect enough to suffer for. So I executed the only logical command available. I went upstairs, put my key on the desk, and quit.  
No backup plan. No savings. Just the sun and the street.

CHAPTER 13: THE COURT AND THE CORPORATION

Then came the Yukos case. And because of where I lived, it became personal. Not because I knew Mikhail Khodorkovsky, but because geography is destiny.

In 2003, the Russian intelligentsia looked at Khodorkovsky not just as an oligarch, but as a prototype of a future President. He was young, he was smart, and he was dangerously rich. We projected our hopes onto him. He was trying to build the first transparent, Western-style corporation in a land of grey schemes. Of course, we weren't naive. We all understood how taxes worked in the 90s. We knew about the "internal offshores." We knew the capital wasn't virgin clean. But we forgave it. Because he was trying to legalize the loot. He was trying to turn "Money" into "Civilization."

The State decided to turn it back into dust. For the rest of the world, the trial was a headline. For me, it was a traffic jam on my daily walk. The Khamovnichesky Court is situated exactly in the middle of my route from the Berezhkovskaya Embankment to Smolenskaya Square. To get to the city center, to my meetings, to my life, I had to physically walk past the dismantling of the country's wealthiest man.

I remember walking across the bridge, the wind from the river cutting through my jacket. I would approach the court and see the crowd. Cameras, supporters, police, the curious. Inside, a man was sitting in a cage. Outside, I was walking to buy groceries or meet a friend. The contrast was jarring. The banality of my personal routine collided with the brutality of history.  
I stopped sometimes. I looked at the building. I understood exactly what I was seeing. It wasn't a trial. It was a public flogging. It was a signal broadcast on all frequencies: "You do not own anything. You are merely leasing your assets from the Kremlin. And the lease can be terminated at any moment."

After the verdict, a heavy silence fell over the city. Big Business shut its mouth. The era of the vocal, ambitious Oligarch was over. The era of the silent, trembling Service Class had begun. I kept walking past the court. The crowds eventually dispersed. The cameras left. But the signal remained in the air, invisible and heavy, like the static before a thunderstorm.

I joined RBC the way I did everything else: by accident, through a connection on LiveJournal. I had two meetings that day. One was with Anton Nossik, who was navigating the new media landscape after LiveJournal was bought by Alexander Mamut. The other was with Ruben Makarov, a manager at RBC. Ruben asked for help. I said yes.

The era of glamour photography was over. I put on a suit. In the mid-2000s, RBC was a pillar of the Russian internet--our version of Bloomberg. It sat in the pantheon of tech giants alongside Yandex and Mail.ru. Yandex was where the dreamers went. RBC was where the money was.  
I remember my interview with HR. "Why do you want to work here?" she asked, pen ready. "Friends asked me to help," I replied. She stopped. That wasn't in the script. I was supposed to talk about "career growth" and "market leadership." I didn't care about that; I just wanted to see how the machine worked.

I started as an Advertising Manager, managing banners on the home page. But in a corporate hierarchy, perception is everything. I was soon promoted to "Director of Integration." The title meant nothing and everything at the same time. I lived within the system--emails, CCs, bureaucracy. But I had three assets the standard corporate employees lacked: I drank with the bosses, I understood technology better than the IT department, and I had no fear.  
Most employees in a Russian corporation are designed to execute, not to think. They keep their heads down to protect their positions. I did the opposite. I spoke. When the Chairman asked a question, I gave an honest answer. In a room full of "yes-men," the truth is a weapon. It made me dangerous, and it made me valuable.

My actual work was analyzing social services. I was obsessed with the XMPP protocol (Jabber). It was a universal language that could unite all messengers into one open network. No walls, just pure connection. I was heavily involved in the redesign of QIP--the most popular message aggregator of that era. I was trying to build bridges.  
But the corporation wanted to build fences. The boom of "Odnoklassniki" (Classmates) had just started. The social network hysteria was everywhere. RBC wanted a piece of the market. They didn't want open protocols; they wanted a captive audience they could control.

It was a failure.  
I learned a fundamental lesson: Big corporations do not create innovation. They are sterile. Real breakthroughs are made by small teams in garages. Corporations can buy success, but they cannot breed it. RBC tried to build a social network by committee. It failed. I watched it happen and I took notes.

CHAPTER 14: THE LONDON SHUTTLE

In the middle of my RBC years, I left. It happened by accident on my birthday. I was not yet a sophisticated traveler; I had only visited Berlin and Israel. When I decided to move, I simply asked the embassies which of them processed visas the fastest. The British were the most efficient. I handed in the papers, received the stamp, and flew.

I arrived with the heavy baggage of a Soviet childhood. I remembered the glossy *Angliya* magazine that used to lie on our coffee table, promising a world of tweed and polite, distant smiles. I knew the schoolboy mantra: "London is the capital of Great Britain." To us, it wasn't a geographical fact; it was a concept of "The Elsewhere."  
I landed, walked into the city, and mentally, I would remain for a decade.

From that first trip, my life split in two. There was the Moscow Anton: aging, cynical, fighting in corporate trenches, inhaling exhaust and ambient aggression. And there was the London Anton: younger, calmer, lighter. I realized quickly that London is the most convenient city on earth. It is built for people.  
London is not a single metropolis; it is a collection of villages welded together by history and the Tube. My village was a small patch of land between Maida Vale and Finchley Road. In a one-mile radius, I had everything: Tesco for sustenance, the Post Office for connection, Regent's Park for air, and Primrose Hill for perspective.

I became a flâneur. I walked twenty, thirty, sometimes forty kilometers a day. It was a form of active meditation. Physical exhaustion successfully killed the mental noise. If I had a problem in Moscow, I walked it off in London. Usually, the solution appeared by the twentieth kilometer. Or the problem simply stopped mattering.

There is a specific skill required to being an intellectual abroad: one must know how to look like a millionaire while having zero pounds in one's pocket. I lived in the center of one of the world's most expensive cities. I drank good wine and smoked hand-rolled cigarettes.  
I felt as if money was lying under my feet. I constantly found coins and banknotes on the street. I even kept a spreadsheet for a year to track these finds. London was the most generous city I had ever known. The conclusion was simple: if you look down, you find value.  
This applied to my finances as well. Problems were often solved by the walk itself. I would leave the house with empty pockets, bump into an acquaintance on a street corner, and return home with a contract. I realized that money is not a static number in a bank account; money is a flow. As long as you keep moving, the flow sustains you. If you stop, you drown.

My trust in the city was truly earned in 2018. One Saturday, I was walking near Tower Bridge and slipped on the wet pavement. I woke up with a broken arm. Usually, this is an immigrant's nightmare: insurance panic, bills, and the crushing weight of bureaucracy. But I walked into Guy's Hospital. There were no questions and no credit cards required. Just polite, efficient care. I walked out with a sling and a realization: civilization isn't just about clean streets; it's about not being afraid to fall.  
I spent the next weeks typing columns with one hand. Two years later, during the lockdown, that broken arm felt like a dress rehearsal for the global fracture we were all about to experience.

I lived everywhere: on friends' couches, in expensive flats, and in cheap B&Bs. When people asked what I did there, I told them the truth: "I live." It was the only honest answer. I wasn't working or studying in the traditional sense. I was just being.  
For a Russian, London offers a drug more potent than heroin: safety. I felt a profound calm. The parks and the flowers drained the Moscow stress away. The city itself felt eternally young. The universities brought in new blood every September. The tourists brought noise. The capital status brought weight. I realized that the "London Me" was the superior version of myself. He didn't age as fast. He didn't look over his shoulder.

Life became a double existence. There was "Here" (the struggle) and "There" (the life). I thought I could commute between these two realities forever. I didn't know yet that history would eventually force me to choose one.  
There is a curious psychological effect to the border--a reset of character that defies logic. Perhaps it was only me, but the moment I cleared passport control at Heathrow, I shed my skin. If I could afford it, I took the Heathrow Express rather than the grim austerity of the Stansted bus. In that transition, I became a different version of myself: tranquil, tolerant, almost benevolent. It was as if the city's inherent politeness was an ambient gas I inhaled at the gate, awakening every dormant virtue I possessed.

It functioned with brutal efficiency in reverse. Upon landing in Russia, the ritual began with the phone. I would switch it on to call my family, to signal my arrival. But as soon as the signal was picked up by the tower, the base changed. My first words into the receiver were usually a torrent of profanity and a general curse directed at the entire planet. Moscow didn't just demand your presence; it demanded your aggression as a survival protocol. You don't arrive in Russia; you prepare for impact.

The social scene was surreal. In Moscow, government meetings would end early just so high-ranking officials, their families, and their nannies could catch the last British Airways flight to London. I looked at that world and knew I didn't want to fall into the "Russian pit"--the gilded ghetto of the diaspora. I wanted to build my own London, a real one. But you cannot build a life in two-week intervals. Every time I had to return to Moscow, whatever I had started to build in London would collapse. I was a commuter between a life that was starting and a life that was ending.  
I once tested the absolute continuity of the city. I walked from Heathrow Airport to Central London. No luggage, no practical reason--just to prove it could be done. The route lay along the Grand Union Canal. It was long, and in parts, shockingly dirty--a side of London the tourists never see. But it proved a point: the city is not a series of disconnected islands. It is a single, walkable organism, provided you have the legs and the stubbornness to navigate.

My real leverage wasn't in reports or social network strategies. It was in my pocket.  
To understand my relationship with technology, you have to go back to 2007. Steve Jobs introduced the first iPhone. It was amazing, but for me, it was primarily a puzzle.  
The first iPhones were locked to AT&T. To use them in Russia, you had to perform surgery. I bought a SIM card copying device--a small gray box--and a stack of blank "Silver Cards" from shady kiosks near the metro. Just like that, I became the most important person in the company.  
The scene repeated weekly. We would be in a boardroom discussing quarterly reports and traffic growth. Serious business. Then, the door would open. A senior board member would walk in. Not an assistant, but the boss himself. He would interrupt the meeting, ignore the agenda, and say: "Anton. We need you."  
Everyone understood. The boss had brought a new iPhone from New York or London.  
I would go to the executive floor and perform the ritual: Jailbreak. Cydia. SIM copy. Activation. I would clone the encrypted data onto my blank card and fool the software into thinking it was on the AT&T network.  
Twenty minutes later, the CEO would have a functioning, unlocked iPhone. He didn't thank me warmly, but he gave the subtle nod of a man who understands leverage. I wasn't just the guy who understood the internet; I was the guy who could unlock the future.  
The device was designed to be closed. We hacked it to make it open. It was a metaphor for the era. I became very good at unlocking iPhones, but I didn't know how to unlock myself. That would take another fifteen years, a war, and an arrest.

2008 was the year the world cracked. The subprime mortgage bubble burst, banks collapsed, and the global economy became a live autopsy. At RBC, the mood was grim. We were cutting budgets, firing staff, and writing reports about the end of capitalism. I had an escape route. Every evening, I would take off my suit, turn off the news, and log into a better jurisdiction: World of Warcraft.  
Azeroth wasn't just a game. It was a perfect behavioral model. In the real world, the rules were messy. You could work hard and still get fired. The logic was broken. In WoW, the logic was absolute. If you wanted routine, you did daily quests. For teamwork and politics, you went on a Raid. For pure capitalism, you played the market at the Auction House. It was a clean system: effort equaled reward. For a mind tired of corporate schizophrenia, it was therapy.

Then I found the ultimate asset: The Spectral Tiger. It came from a loot code in a physical card game. It was rare and expensive. Crucially, the Tiger gave you no actual advantage. It wasn't faster than a standard horse. It didn't add stamina or strength. Its utility was exactly zero. Its only function was Status.  
I bought it on eBay for $500. In 2008, spending five hundred dollars on a digital cat was considered insanity. You could buy a used car or a lot of vodka for that money. I made the transaction without hesitation.  
I did it to stand in Ironforge, the main hub for the Alliance. I would summon the Tiger and just park in the center of the main square. I stood there doing absolutely nothing. Other players would run past, stop, and look. They knew the price and they knew the rarity. It was the digital equivalent of parking a yellow Ferrari on Red Square. You don't drive it to get groceries; you drive it to show that you can.  
While the real economy was burning outside my window, I sat on my glowing ghost tiger, radiating success. That account likely still exists on Blizzard's servers. The password is lost and the email is forgotten, but the Tiger is still there in the code. Eternal, useless, and beautiful. It was my first real lesson in the modern economy: Utility is for the poor. The real markup is always on the Air.

The account with the Spectral Tiger is likely still alive somewhere in the database. Of course, since it's no longer tied to a physical card, its market value has dropped. But as an asset, it served its purpose. It taught me that in a collapsing economy, a digital ghost can be more stable than a bank account. It was a proof of concept for everything that would happen ten years later.

CHAPTER 15: THE GOAT AND THE EXPERT

This story illustrates the atmosphere of those years. In 2009, the financial crisis was raging, but the internet bubble was still inflating. LiveJournal--the parliament of our generation--was bought by the businessman Alexander Mamut. Anton Nossik became the new boss.  
We were invited to the acquisition party. I was at lunch with my RBC colleagues, and we didn't want to show up empty-handed. We needed a gesture. LiveJournal's mascot was Frank the Goat. I looked at my plate and said: "Why don't we gift Nossik a goat?" Someone asked if I meant a statue. "No," I said. "A real one."  
My colleagues had connections in the countryside. Logistics were arranged. That evening, a large Jeep pulled up to our glass-and-steel office. Inside the trunk was a live goat. We drove to the party with a clear conscience.  
The event was in the center of Moscow, on Tverskoy Boulevard. The media elite were all there. I walked toward the venue leading a goat on a rope. The security guards had no protocol for livestock on a guest list. I walked up to Nossik, handed him the rope, and said: "This is for you."  
We didn't speak for two years after that. It was too absurd, even for him. But eventually, we returned to a friendly relationship. It was a time when bringing a farm animal to a black-tie event was seen as "performance art" rather than hooliganism.

The 2008 crisis cleaned the market. It washed away the fake experts. At RBC, the mood was dark. Budgets were cut and people were fired. The party was over. But in the middle of this collapse, I received an internal award: "Best Analyst of RBC 2009."  
It was ironic. I wasn't a financier or an economist. I was the guy with the goat and the jailbroken iPhone. But I won because I refused to panic. While others wrote hysterical reports based on emotion, I treated the crisis as data. I called things by their names. If a project was dead, I said so. If traffic was fake, I showed the numbers. In a country built on hierarchy, objectivity is a superpower.  
I accepted the award, but I knew I was leaving. The "Best Analyst" badge was just the final stamp on my exit visa from the corporate world. I had outgrown the suit.

But reality is rarely as seamless as a status update. My life between Moscow and London became a war of attrition against bureaucracy. When I left RBC, I lost my "Certificate of Employment"--the holy grail for visa officers. Without corporate cover, I was just a Russian male of military age with an irregular income. To the consular algorithm, I was a risk.  
To complicate matters, my Israeli citizenship was limited. I held a Lesse-Passe (the Red Passport). It allowed me to travel to Europe, but the UK border remained closed. I had to fight for a visa using my Russian passport.  
The UK visa center became stricter. My passport began to accumulate refusal stamps. One, two, three, four. The fifth refusal is etched in my memory.  
I received the notification and walked to the visa center. I opened the envelope and found another standard letter of rejection. I stood in the middle of a grey Moscow street and cried. It wasn't grief; it was rage and impotence. I had the money and the time. The city I loved was waiting for me. But a piece of paper said "No."

The bureaucracy was an insult. The UK visa form actually asks if you are a person of 'good character.' Having to constantly prove my existence with paperwork felt as absurd as being forced to defend an opinion with a graph. I wasn't doing anything illegal; I was just visiting, spending modest amounts of money, and walking. I found the necessity to pay for the right to travel and collect documents for every trip offensive. But I swallowed my pride for the sake of access. To me, a visa wasn't just a sticker; it was a subscription to a better version of reality.  
It was a brutal lesson: You can have a global consciousness, but if you have the wrong paper, you are still a prisoner of your geography. Eventually, the problem resolved itself--bureaucracy is a tide that goes out as mysteriously as it comes in--but the fear of a closed border never left me.

In those years, money was easy. It fell on anyone who was doing something--anything. I wasn't trying to earn all the money in the world, and I didn't really think about it. Between speaking gigs, business trips, short consulting projects, and media columns, the flow was steady. I wasn't chasing wealth; it was just a byproduct of being active. It was enough to sustain a normal life without a fixed salary.

The market was crashing, and everyone was looking for a benchmark. They didn't realize that in a storm, the only hard currency is clarity. I didn't win because I knew more economics than the professors. I won because I trusted my own optics more than the general hysteria.  
This has been my default setting since childhood: my view is the most solid object in the room. I don't operate on faith, ideology, or 'moral compasses.' Those are volatile variables. I operate on my own output. My opinion is the hardest asset I possess, and unlike the Ruble or the Index, it doesn't devalue when the news gets bad.

The defining conflict in Russia--one that you feel with visceral clarity--is that politics has lost its tether to reality. To me, a messaging app or a new interface remains a miracle. Elon Musk launches satellites; autonomous taxis conquer cities; the iPhone is a device more potent than any wand in the Harry Potter universe. The iPad alone has rendered the laptop and the PC obsolete for me for years. Then there is Artificial Intelligence. The list of wonders is exhausting. That is one side of the scale.  
On the other, you have elderly idiots reciting the mantra of "sovereignty." It is a farce--especially when you know that the best wine is in Italy, the best cars are in Germany, the finest cheese is in Switzerland, and the artists are still in France. It is a simplification, perhaps, but when you feel the sheer velocity and potential of a globalized world, listening to sermons on "traditional values" is, to put it mildly, laughable. Or it would be, if it weren't the actual reality governing the country.

When RBC collapsed in 2009, I didn't panic. I was in London when the news came. By then, I was already living in "Shuttle Mode"--commuting between the adrenaline of Moscow and the sanity of Primrose Hill. The arrangement was comfortable for both sides: they paid me, and I didn't bother them. Then the bankruptcy hit. Someone from HR sent me a polite message: "Anton, this is getting excessive. When you return to Moscow, please come by to officially resign." There was no drama and no accusations. It was just the bureaucratic tidying-up of a company that was falling apart.  
I didn't look for a job. Instead, I invented a status: "Independent Internet Expert."  
To understand the context, you have to look at how technology and society collided. For a long time, the state had no interest in the internet. It was almost entirely private. There were battles over infrastructure, but there was no real politics involved. Officials were a rarity online; they were an exotic species.  
Then, it became clear that the internet was the future. The authorities realized it could be dangerous if left uncontrolled. Following the masses, the bureaucrats flooded the web. There is an important distinction to make here: the interests of a typical deputy or government representative are limited to roads and hospitals. They had no technical education and no understanding of how the network functioned. To be fair, they still don't.  
My phone didn't ring because people were suddenly curious about the internet. It rang because another state representative had gone on the news and said something incredibly stupid. I was the one who had to explain, in tedious detail, why the official's statement was technically illiterate.  
However, the real reason for my success was never my deep technological knowledge. It was simpler: I was polite, punctual, and reliable. I was always available for a comment, I joked with the editors and producers, and I was energetic on air. In a world of grumpy experts, I was easy to work with.  
It was a role built on a specific gap in the market. The engineers couldn't speak human, and the humans couldn't understand code. I stood in the middle. I had the face for TV and the rhetoric for the microphone. I could explain blockchain to a grandmother--not literally, but metaphorically. I could translate censorship into language a liberal would understand, and digital sovereignty into language a bureaucrat would pretend to understand.  
The timing was perfect. The internet was rising, and the State was just beginning to stare at the screen with suspicion. They needed someone to explain what they were looking at. I wasn't a celebrity; I was something more useful: a Source.

Let's clarify what "working as an expert" actually entails.  
First, it is not a job. There is no payroll. In Russia, opinions are free; the market rate for truth is zero. The only exception was the BBC. Those interactions were pleasant, particularly the ritual of arriving in London, walking into the old Bush House, and collecting an envelope with a small sum of cash. It felt tangible. Honest.  
Second, there is the utility of fame. Deep down, despite the public persona, I possess a core of modesty, perhaps even crippling shyness. I lack the Western skill of the elevator pitch--that breezy ability to say, "Hi, I'm Anton, I do X, how are you?" I never learned how to impose myself on others.  
Television solved this friction. Visibility acted as a social prosthetic. When your face is on the screen and your voice is on the business radio frequency, the introduction is already made. People approach you. At parties, hosts are eager to present you: "Look! It's him. The guy from the news." It was efficient. It allowed me to be in the room without the awkwardness of having to knock on the door.  
As for the substance--did I actually change anything?  
In the beginning, perhaps. There was a time when a sharp comment on a morning radio show could make a deputy hesitate, or at least stall a bad law. But entropy is relentless. With every year, the window of influence narrowed.  
For a decade, I essentially answered a single question: "Can they block the internet?" My answer was a static "No," but the rhetoric devolved along with the regime.  
Phase One: Technical. I explained protocols, TCP/IP, and the impossibility of filtering a river with a sieve. Phase Two: Legal. I argued that censorship violated the Constitution. Phase Three: Ad Hominem. To add heat to the dying fire, I switched to personalities. "Criminals are deciding how you live," I would say. This wasn't hyperbole. One of the key initiators of internet censorship was Andrei Lugovoy--a man accused by British authorities of poisoning Alexander Litvinenko with polonium. He found asylum in the State Duma. Calling him a criminal wasn't an insult; it was a statement of fact.  
By the end of my "career," I had abandoned logic entirely and moved to direct insults. But you cannot shame a wall.  
Ultimately, the fatigue came not from the failure, but from the lack of agency. I was living on someone else's agenda. My phone didn't ring because I had created something beautiful; it rang because the State had done something stupid. I was a secondary character in their script, a reactive mechanism triggered by their incompetence. It wasn't my life; it was just a commentary on theirs.

CHAPTER 16: THE BROADCAST AND THE BAR

I moved from local tabloids to the global giants: BBC, Euronews, and the *Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung*. It wasn't dozens of appearances; it was hundreds. I lost count. The topics were predictable: internet censorship, new services, and bans. For Euronews, I remember explaining the absurdity of Russia trying to block Telegram--a classic example of the State fighting a technology it didn't understand.  
Giving a comment to the world press wasn't an achievement; it was just part of the morning routine. A producer would call, I would find a quiet corner, open Skype, and talk for five minutes. The segment would air, and I would move on to the next call.

I secured a column on Radio Mayak (*The Lighthouse*)--the oldest, most iconic station in the country. The voice that had announced the death of Stalin was now broadcasting my thoughts on digital trends. I don't remember exactly how I got the gig; they had likely been calling me for expert comments so often that someone suggested making it a regular thing. The format was simple: a weekly segment on the news of the blogosphere. What was trending, who was fighting, and what the government was trying to ban that week. It was a comfortable rhythm--predictable and visible enough to keep my name circulating.

It was an era of speed. Opportunities didn't knock; they fell out of the sky. One afternoon, I was standing on a friend's balcony in London, smoking. I was bored. I pulled out my phone and typed a single sentence into Facebook: "I want to work on TV."  
Minutes later, a comment popped up from Ekaterina Shergova. I knew her from the network--part of the same loose circle of media people who floated between Moscow and the West. She didn't ask for a CV or schedule a meeting. She just said: "Come."

Just like that, I was hosting my own program, *Freedom of Access*, on the Top Secret channel. The format was straightforward: one hour with a guest, covering tech, culture, and politics. I wasn't performing; I was just talking. It was the same conversations I'd been having in cafés and on balconies, but now with cameras.  
The show lasted less than a year, but not because of censorship. The channel was technically incompetent. YouTube was rising, and they couldn't figure out how to publish content online. For me, that was a dealbreaker. If the show wasn't accessible on-demand and people couldn't share clips, if it existed only within the limits of broadcast television, there was no point.  
I had learned what I needed to learn about the process. I understood the mechanics of TV production. I knew how to hold a shot, how to keep a conversation moving, and how to edit in real-time by controlling the flow of speech. The duration didn't matter. The fact that I could make things happen with a status update did.

This was roughly 2010-2013--the golden years, in retrospect. The country was stable. The economy was growing. You could still say things on air without wondering if someone was keeping a file on you. I never felt pressure. I had always maintained a parallel life: one foot in Moscow, one foot in London. If things got bad, I could leave. That freedom was a luxury most people didn't have.  
Looking back, I realize I was living in a bubble. Not a financial bubble--I was never rich--but a bubble of freedom. I was visible enough to have influence but not visible enough to be a threat. I could criticize the government as long as I framed it as tech commentary rather than politics. I could travel freely because I had the Israeli passport in my pocket. I thought this balance could last forever. I thought I was smart enough to stay afloat without getting crushed.  
I didn't realize yet that the wave was about to break, and when it did, there would be no safe shore to swim to. But in those years, standing on balconies and posting status updates that turned into TV shows, I felt invincible. Or at least, I felt like I had figured out the game.

My only real qualification was my refusal to believe the default settings.  
Curiosity is not just a hobby; in an authoritarian state, it is a survival mechanism. When the TV says "A", you must ask "Why not B?". When the boss says "This is impossible," you must ask "How does it work?".  
Arguing with reality is the only way to understand it. I spent my life dismantling assertions--technical, political, social. Not because I wanted to be difficult (though I am), but because blind acceptance is the first step to becoming furniture. And in Russia, furniture usually gets broken. To survive, you must remain a moving target. You must question everything.

My methodology was never deep craftsmanship; it was speed and *Chuika*--that untranslatable Russian gut instinct. The Specialist spends ten years learning how the engine works. I spend two weeks reading the manual, subscribing to the top industry reports, and mapping the terrain.  
It's a simple algorithm: consume the data, spot the patterns, and jump in before the experts finish their coffee. I realized early on that you don't need to know every detail to be effective; you just need to know enough to navigate. While the academics were debating definitions, I was already closing deals. It's far from deep expertise, but in a fast-moving world, depth is often just gravity.

To understand the social stratification of the era, you had to navigate the Holy Trinity of Moscow nightlife.  
First, Denis Simachev Shop & Bar. Friday night. A fusion of Journalism Faculty students, mid-level managers from oil-adjacent corporations, and golden youth. It was an oasis of high-end chaos on Stoleshnikov Lane. You pushed through the crowd, greeted Denis at the DJ decks, checked the bathroom for old girlfriends, and felt like you were at the center of the universe.  
Then, there was Mayak (*The Lighthouse*). The anti-glamour headquarters. The legends said you could find the editors of Kommersant or scandalous directors drinking here. And you could. You would see familiar faces in the mirror of the restroom, wondering how you were still communicating after that amount of vodka. It was the place where the intelligentsia went to decompose gracefully.  
But the only truth existed on The Kitchen.  
You could be at the hottest party, but the real Moscow happened when you ended up in someone's kitchen at 4:00 AM. Tea, cigarettes, and philosophy. We sat there, dismissing the glitter of Simachev and the noise of the deals, convincing ourselves that this--the talk, the smoke, the intimacy--was the only reality. We thought we were smart. We thought the world outside was fake. We didn't know yet that the world outside was coming for the kitchen, too.

There is a specific Moscow ritual: The Morning Call.  
I woke up hungover after a night at Mayak. The phone was ringing. Friends, relatives, voices trembling with relief: 'Are you okay? You're alive.' I grunted into the receiver, wanting coffee, not understanding the hysteria.  
Then I opened the news. Explosion in the Metro. Panic. The internet spinning like a centrifuge of fear. For an hour, the city realizes it is at war.  
But the terrifying part is not the explosion. It is the Amnesia.  
By the next morning, it was just a bad dream. The Metro was full again. The same people pushing each other on the escalators. We smiled nervously, but we kept moving. If you didn't check the news, the city looked exactly the same. The flow of people and money washed away the blood instantly.  
For the majority of Muscovites, every day was both the First Day (a fresh start) and the Last Day (because you might blow up). So we lived at maximum velocity. We drank harder, partied louder, and ignored the reality. We treated terror not as a tragedy, but as a weather condition. Bad forecast today? Take an umbrella. Bombing today? Call mom, then go to the bar.

CHAPTER 17: THE ANALYST AND THE HERITAGE

I returned to professional writing the same way I did everything else: by accident. My previous experiment with CDPro had been for fun and pocket money. I didn't take it seriously. I viewed the text as code--something to be compiled and executed, not crafted for eternity. But year by year, my media optics were sharpening.

One day, surfing the debris of the Russian internet, I stumbled upon a new project: *Private Correspondent* (Chaskor.ru). It was an anomaly. While the rest of the web was racing toward clickbait and short attention spans, Chaskor was publishing long, deep, academic-level texts. It felt like a university department that had accidentally wandered onto the internet.  
I wrote a post on my blog, half-cynical, half-admiring: "Unbelievable. Someone is running media for the intelligentsia. The texts are long, deep, and brilliant. But I don't understand--who is this for, exactly? Except for me?"

The algorithm of fate responded immediately. *Private Correspondent* was established by Ivan Zassoursky. If the name doesn't ring a bell, you don't know the architecture of the Russian media elite. Ivan is the grandson of Yasen Zasoursky, the legendary Dean of the Faculty of Journalism at Moscow State University. Yasen was the "Granite" of Soviet Journalism--the man who taught the entire industry how to write. Ivan had his own Department of New Media and Communications.  
It was a collision of legacies. The grandson of the Dean meeting the great-grandson of the Sculptor. Ivan read my post. He didn't take offense; he saw an asset. He invited me to the office. "You should write for us," he said, cutting through the formalities. "About the internet. You deeply understand the theme, and you have the skills. It will be successful."

So, I went back to the keyboard. But I set a strict internal boundary. I never identified myself as a "Journalist." Why? First: I lack professional convictions. Journalism assumes a mission--to serve the public, to uncover the truth, to suffer for the story. I had no mission. I had interests. Second: Journalists, historically, do not earn money. They earn bylines. I preferred to dream about a rich lifestyle, and the economics of journalism did not support the lifestyle I felt entitled to. Third: I am an Analyst. A Columnist. A Publicist. A journalist records reality. An analyst deconstructs it.  
I wasn't there to report the news. I was there to explain why the news didn't matter, and what was happening underneath the hood. Ivan gave me the platform. I gave him the "Bureaucratic Magic" of explaining the digital shift to the academic elite. It was another step away from the office cubicle and toward the Ether.

This craving for high-density information didn't come from nowhere. It was a habit cultivated in London. Whenever I was in the UK, I indulged in a specific ritual. On Sunday mornings, I would take two pounds, walk to the newsstand near the Tube, and buy the physical edition of *The Sunday Times*. It was a heavy, tactile brick of paper. I would spend three hours dissecting it. I read everything--not just the Politics and Business sections, which were my trade, but the Gardening, the Sport, the Culture. I didn't care about cricket or hydrangeas, but I cared about the quality of the narrative. It was the best edition in the world.  
My love for *The Sunday Times* was also personal. In my previous life as a photographer, I had contributed to it. My photos had graced those pages--a portrait of a high-profile lawyer, a shot of Zhanna Nemtsova (the daughter of Boris Nemtsov) standing on Red Square. Seeing my work printed on that paper felt different than seeing it on a screen. It felt legitimate. It felt like history.

This addiction to quality media has deep roots. It goes back to the "Stalinka" on Berezhkovskaya. My family subscribed to everything: *Koster* (Bonfire), *Yuniy Tekhnik* (Young Technician). We kept archives of *USSR in Construction*--propaganda, yes, but propaganda designed by Alexander Rodchenko. I grew up looking at the best avant-garde layout in history. I learned early that the medium is the message.  
When the iPad appeared, I was one of the first subscribers to the digital edition of *The Sunday Times*. I transferred the ritual from paper to glass, but the principle remained the same. Even today, I prefer *The Financial Times* and Bloomberg. I do not read the yellow press. I do not consume junk food, and I do not consume junk data. In a world of noise, the most expensive asset is a clean signal.

Looking back, the tragedy wasn't that I failed. The tragedy was that I refused to land.  
The 2000s and early 2010s were a historical anomaly--a long, warm autumn before the winter. It was the only time in Russian history when the State stepped back just enough to let us breathe. We mistook this pause for freedom.  
I lived through this era like a tourist walking through a buffet. I took a bite of television, a sip of corporate PR, a taste of the art market. I moved from project to project, driven not by ambition, but by boredom. As soon as I understood how a system worked, I lost interest. The moment a job threatened to become a "career"--with its routines, politics, and steady paycheck--I ran.

Let's be honest about the economics: I never made "real" money. I never saw the life-changing bonuses or the heavy envelopes that defined the era for others. I earned exactly enough to sustain the movement. A fee came in, a ticket was bought. The balance returned to zero.  
I didn't buy apartments. I didn't build a dacha. I didn't accumulate the heavy, physical anchors that tether a man to the ground. I bought wine. I bought time. And mostly, I bought the right to say "no" to boring work, even if it meant being broke a month later.  
I thought I was being smart. I thought I was choosing freedom over gravity. While my peers were mortgaging their futures for concrete boxes on the outskirts of Moscow, I was living in the ether.  
But gravity always wins in the end. When the weather changed, when the "Fat Years" ended and the wind turned cold, I looked around and realized the truth. I had spent fifteen years working, but I had built nothing. I had a backpack full of stories and a passport full of stamps, but I had nowhere to hide. I was a man with a thousand keys, but not a single door of my own.

I remember when the timeline locked. September 24, 2011.  
I was in St. Petersburg for a conference. Back in my hotel room, I turned on the TV and watched Dmitry Medvedev and Vladimir Putin announce the "Castling." They were swapping roles. The conclusion was instant: it was the beginning of the end. Medvedev had missed his historical window to fire Putin. The door was closed.  
Later that night, I met Vlad Tsyplukhin, the press secretary for VKontakte. He gave me a tour of their office--the center of the Russian social web--and then we went to a bar. We drank whiskey. "It's over," I told him. "The system is dead." He didn't believe me. We operated on different levels of optimism. But knowing the future and acting on it are two different things. Intellectual clarity doesn't require physical action. I finished my drink, flew back to Moscow, and continued my life.

My relationship with the 2011 protests--the "Snow Revolution"--was a binary system. The romantic in me felt a flicker of hope. The analyst watched the math, and the math said no.  
I didn't go to Bolotnaya Square. Strategically, it was a trap--an island surrounded by police cordons. Instead, whenever something happened in the center, on Tverskaya Street or Manezhnaya Square, I went to observe. For the Moscow intelligentsia, a protest was the ultimate networking event. It was the Vanity Fair of the opposition: smart people, the creative class, and beautiful women. I walked through the crowd as a flâneur, not a participant. I wasn't part of the collective body; I was inspecting it.  
I don't traffic in 'moral imperatives.' I don't have a guilt complex that drives me to the barricades. My operating system is simpler: Is this interesting? Is this useful? Is this safe?  
Standing in the freezing cold to get beaten by a policeman offered a negative ROI on all three counts. Watching it in HD with a glass of Shiraz was efficient. People mistake this for cynicism or cowardice. It isn't. It's just a refusal to let external emotions dictate my logistics. I don't need to be 'good' for history. I need to be comfortable for myself.

I had a rule: self-preservation above heroism. I am afraid of the police. I have no desire to be detained or processed by the system. I preferred being an observer, not a martyr. To ensure I didn't do anything stupid, I developed a safety protocol. I hacked my own biology.  
When a major rally was announced, I would buy a bottle of red wine, go home, and open it. This was my insurance. In Russia, being drunk at a political rally is an "aggravating circumstance" that gives the police a reason to destroy you. By drinking that wine, I created a physical firewall. I made it impossible for myself to leave the house.

The Pussy Riot prayer--"Mother of God, Drive Putin Away"--didn't work. That alone cast doubt on the divine. But the result was clear: it was a foundation stone for the "Orthodox Iran" Russia was becoming.

My life wasn't just online. Being a "blogger" gave me mobility. But to use it, I needed a product. In 2012, I published a book: *Sergey Merkurov. Memories*.  
To understand my approach, you have to understand the context. Usually, dealing with cultural heritage in Russia is a depressing business. It is the world of aging heirs--grandmothers and grandfathers living in dusty apartments--who spend their pensions to publish small-circulation albums that nobody buys. They are driven by sentiment, duty, and a desperate desire to keep the past alive.  
I looked at this landscape and realized immediately: This is not my path.

I established a set of strict rules for dealing with the dead. First, it must be a market product. No "vanity publishing" on my own dime. If I find a sponsor--great. If not--we wait. Heritage is not a charity; it is a business. If it's not a product, it shouldn't exist.  
Second, unlike most heirs, I was young, energetic, and tech-savvy. I had no special emotional trembling before the "Great Ancestor." I never knew Sergey Merkurov personally; he died thirty years before I was born. To me, he wasn't "Grandpa"; he was a historical figure, a brand. Publishing the book was simply paying a debt. My grandfather had spent years compiling the archive; by publishing it, I finished his work. From that point on, I could work with the legacy on my own terms.

This pragmatism extended to the subject matter itself. Take Lenin. My great-grandfather carved the definitive image of Lenin for the Empire. For some, Lenin is a hero; for others, a murderer. For me? He is nobody. He is just a historical character. I honestly don't care about the ideology. I look at the works strictly as art objects. Lenin might have been a monster, but the monuments my great-grandfather made were excellent. Stone doesn't care about morality, and neither do I. I evaluate the composition, not the politics.  
Third, why turn to heritage at all? Honestly? Laziness. Usually, I returned to family affairs during the pauses--when the consulting gigs dried up or the internet bored me. Why invent a startup from scratch when you have a massive, unrecognized archive gathering dust in the closet? It was the path of least resistance. The content was already there; it just needed packaging.

CHAPTER 19: THE DUMA AND THE DOSE

The State Duma building on Okhotny Ryad has a specific smell. It is a cocktail of boiled cabbage from the Soviet canteen, expensive Italian cologne, and fear.  
I had a pass to the backstage of this legislative machine. I was naive enough to think I was going there to explain that banning IP addresses is like trying to catch water with a fishing net. I thought I could make them understand. I didn't realize that understanding was irrelevant.

I started talking about mirrors, VPNs, and the Streisand effect.

"But we can report that it's blocked?" he interrupted.

But the real revelation didn't happen in the office. It happened in the car.  
After the meeting, a rising star of the apparatus offered me a lift. We got into his black Audi A8. The driver turned on the blue light--the *migalka*--parting the Moscow traffic instantly.

"Yes."

I got out at the next metro station. The Audi sped away, immune to traffic, immune to laws, immune to reality. I realized then that the system was broken beyond repair. The people running the machine didn't believe in the machine. They were just stripping it for parts before the inevitable crash.

My moral compass was calibrated differently. I intuitively divided people not by the Penal Code, but into "good" and "bad" based on how they treated me. To a Western reader, this might sound savage, but I was largely indifferent to fraud, corruption, and white-collar crime. I never witnessed the crimes personally, but I understood perfectly well that the capital sitting across the table wasn't exactly clean.  
But first, they treated me well. And second, I rarely saw true evil in them. What I saw was opportunism. Screaming "Thief!" is a luxury for the common man. But try to put yourself in that position. "Judge not, that ye be not judged." Given the breadth of my circle and my access to the backstage of so many lives, I knew that reality is always more complex than the headline.  
It is a complex existential knot I have no desire to untie. It usually stems from the accusation: "How could you shake hands with him?" Well, I could. It was my life, my choice, and ultimately, I am the one who has to live with it.

My life as a consultant occasionally placed me in rooms where the future of the state was being debated--often with more ego than intellect. One such encounter was with Mikhail Mishustin, then the head of the Federal Tax Service and now the Prime Minister.  
He was a man famously preoccupied with his public image. I met him as part of a small circle of "digital experts" brought in to provide a veneer of modernity to the tax office. Unlike my peers, I felt no need to flatter him. We fell into a debate about the legalization of foreign bank accounts; Russia was just beginning to establish information exchange protocols with other nations at the time.  
"You'll never find them," I said, with a touch of malice.  
"Oh, we certainly will," he replied, smiling.  
History, as it often does in Russia, settled the debate with a sledgehammer. Following the invasion of Ukraine in 2022, all international data sharing ceased. The search was over before it began, rendered moot by geopolitics.

Our second and final meeting was more surreal. It took place some time later in the Hermitage Garden, a pleasant park directly opposite the headquarters of the Moscow Criminal Investigation Department. I was there with an acquaintance. We had just shared a joint behind some bushes--a small, private rebellion against the surrounding architecture of law and order.  
I emerged from the thicket, parched and seeking water, only to walk straight into Mishustin.  
Surprisingly, he remembered me. Without missing a beat, he resumed our discussion about bank accounts. The formal nature of the event prevented the conversation from dragging on, for which I was grateful, given my compromised state.  
Despite the obvious ideological divide, he left me with a positive impression. In a system often defined by grey, stagnant mediocrity, he possessed a distinct, focused competence. He was a professional, even if we were playing for different teams.

I have always navigated the world on the assumption of absolute equality. I simply do not possess the psychological apparatus for any other mode of communication. Whether I was speaking to a government minister, a billionaire, or a technician, the frequency remained the same. This isn't a practiced technique or a form of performance; it is a default setting. If the dialogue is not peer-to-peer, it ceases to be a dialogue and becomes a lecture or a script--neither of which interests me.

In Russian, it's *Bydlo*, *Babki*, and *Bureaucracy*. The three Bs. This is not a local phenomenon; it functions in every jurisdiction from Washington to Canberra. Watch the hands.

They look at the internet, and their only reflex is to ban it--"for the children," naturally, and eventually for everyone else.

In this sense, the utopian ideals of a brave new world ushered in by technology have not just been challenged; they have been liquidated over the last few years.

CHAPTER 20: THE EMBASSY AND THE JESTER

The early 2000s were a blur of velocity. While the country was slowly freezing over, I was fully integrated into the global context. In Moscow, this cosmopolitan status expressed itself through a specific currency: Embassy Access.

CHAPTER 21: THE ZEROING AND THE SILENCE

Then came "The Zeroing" (*Obnulenie*). In essence, the rewriting of the Constitution to reset Putin's presidential terms was a coup d'état staged as a theatrical performance. CHAPTER 22: THE VISA AND THE WALK

I didn't go to London for culture. Or for history. Or for any of the romantic reasons people like to list in essays about self-discovery. As soon as the landing gear touches the runway in Heathrow, Moscow becomes a fiction. You can't explain it to anyone. You can't even explain it to yourself. It was just a hallucination that lasted a week.

CHAPTER 23: THE WITCH AND THE GRANITE

Once, I stumbled into history by accident. It was April 2013. I was in London, doing my usual routine--walking until my thoughts cleared. And then Margaret Thatcher died.  
Her death was unexpected for me, but I couldn't just skip it. This wasn't a news event you could scroll past. This was a State Funeral. The last of its kind. I walked to the route. I expected crowds. Tens of thousands. A nation frozen in mourning for the woman who had defined an era. What I saw instead was... nothing.

I mean, of course there were people. Thousands, technically. But they were sparse, scattered, polite. It was a spectacle with no emotional charge. The scale of the event--the soldiers, the gun carriage, the pomp--completely dwarfed the human response. It felt like a state ceremony performed for the cameras, not for the citizens.  
I stood there, watching the procession, when a TV crew appeared. A journalist shoved a microphone in my face. "What do you think about this?" he asked. The subtext was clear: the controversy was the cost. I didn't hesitate. "I hope it's the last time," I said. The journalist blinked. He wasn't sure if I meant "the last State Funeral" or "the last time we waste money like this." I didn't clarify.

But the streets told a different story than the television. All over London, the graffiti bloomed: "THE WITCH IS DEAD." It wasn't just angry teenagers. It was a collective exorcism. CHAPTER 24: THE CONCRETE AND THE HALLUCINATION

CHAPTER 25: THE MANSION AND THE MESSAGE

But I wasn't just observing the infrastructure; I was regulating it. I sat on the Accreditation Commission of the Coordination Center for .RU domains. While politicians were screaming about sovereignty on TV, we were quietly deciding who actually owned the keys to the Russian internet.

CHAPTER 26: THE BRIDGE AND THE STONE

When Boris Nemtsov was shot, I was, as usual, in my fortress of solitude. Just chilling. The notification lit up my phone. Four shots in the back. Bolshoy Moskvoretsky Bridge.

It was unbelievable. Not just the violence, but the geography. They didn't kill him in a dark alley. They killed him on the Main Stage. Right there, against the backdrop of St. Basil's Cathedral and the Kremlin walls. The most surveillance-heavy square meter in the entire country. The Federal Protective Service (FSO) monitors every pixel of that space. Yet, in that specific second, the cameras turned away. The System didn't malfunction; it performed a deliberate edit of reality.

I was shocked. Nemtsov wasn't just a competitor to Vladimir Putin; he was his biological opposite. Where the President was grey, bureaucratic, and erased, Nemtsov was technicolor. He was tall, handsome, loud. He loved women, he loved windsurfing, he loved the chaotic energy of the 90s. He was a rock star in a room full of funeral directors. I didn't know him personally--our trajectories never crossed physically--but in Moscow, the circle is tight. We shared friends. We shared the same restaurants. We breathed the same air.

My first thought was cold and immediate: It was a direct order. Or, perhaps, a "birthday gift" from a zealous vassal trying to please the Monarch. In a high-functioning dictatorship, the difference between an order and a wish is negligible. We still don't know the full truth. We probably never will. But the message was written in blood on the wet pavement: The era of "Hybrid Autocracy" is over. The era of Terror has begun.

I stayed home that night. I analyzed the news. The System had just killed the most alive person in politics. And the city... the city just kept driving across the bridge. The traffic didn't stop. The heart rate of the capital didn't spike.

That night, watching the traffic flow over the spot where he died, I archived my belief in politics. I realized that you cannot fix a system that has root access to the physical reality. I stopped looking for leaders. I turned to code.

I became obsessed with the Mesh. The logic was simple: The government was learning how to use the "Kill Switch." They controlled the ISPs, the cables, the servers. If they wanted silence, they pulled the plug. I was looking for a network that had no plug. That's how I found FireChat.

The technology was developed by Open Garden. The vision was pure anarchist beauty: peer-to-peer communication. No internet required. No cellular towers. Your phone talks to my phone via Bluetooth and Wi-Fi, and my phone talks to the next one. A daisy chain of devices. A human router. Christophe Daligault, the Frenchman behind it, pitched it as a festival toy. He saw it as a solution for Burning Man or Coachella. I saw it as a survival protocol.

I ran the operation from my existing base on Malaya Bronnaya--that same loft with the white walls and the view of the Patriarch's Ponds where I spent my days. It wasn't a formal headquarters; it was just where I held court. I became the Evangelist of the Mesh in Russia. I toured the universities. I sat at dinners with activists and journalists. I ran the same slide deck, over and over again. The press loved it. The narrative of an "Unstoppable Network" was sexy. We got excellent coverage. But let's be honest: we didn't break the App Store. The download charts remained indifferent to our revolution.

But the real clash happened over hardware. Open Garden had developed a device called "GreenStone." It was a small plastic pebble, unassuming, equipped with a Bluetooth module and memory. The concept was brilliant: a digital dead drop. It sat there, caching messages from passing phones and relaying them to the next device that came within range. It extended the mesh, bridging the gaps between people. I asked Christophe to send me samples. He put five of them in a box and sent them via standard mail.

A month later, my phone rang. It was the Customs Terminal at Sheremetyevo-2. I drove to the airport. It wasn't a clerk waiting for me; it was an officer from the "Organs." The conversation was a loop of absurdity. He held the small green plastic shape in his hand like it was a piece of alien technology.

"What is this?" he asked.

"It's a prototype. For extending Bluetooth range," I explained.

He didn't blink. "Why is a Western company sending you encryption devices for free? Who are you? What is your relationship with them?"

Trying to explain the concept of "Beta Testing" or "Brand Evangelism" to a man whose job is to hunt spies was useless. To him, a "GreenStone" was obviously a spy rock. I left empty-handed. The five stones remained in the custody of the Russian Federation, presumably filed under "Suspicious Foreign Objects."

But here is the punchline of the Russian logistical reality: A month later, Christophe sent another batch. This time, he used a private courier--FedEx or DHL, I don't remember. They arrived at my door in three days. No questions. No FSB. The State can stop the mail, but it waves through the premium courier.

Ultimately, however, entropy took over. The technology was beautiful on a whiteboard in San Francisco. In the physical reality of Moscow, it failed. The topology of the city was hostile. Moscow is vast. The distances are imperial. The walls are thick concrete. People are cold. They don't cluster; they disperse. And the hardware couldn't keep up. The protocol drained batteries in two hours. The user interface was ugly. FireChat never became a product. It remained a hypothesis.

But for me, the failure was valuable. It taught me two axioms:

Architecture is politics. If there is a server, there is a censor. Freedom is not a law; it is a topology.

Comfort kills resistance. People will not choose freedom if it is inconvenient. Even if you give them a perfect, unbreakable tool, they will still use Telegram. Why? Because Telegram has funny stickers. The masses do not want to be safe; they want to be entertained.

CHAPTER 27: THE NANNY, THE PIKACHU, AND THE PORSCHE

The endless stream of projects--large, small, irrelevant--always seemed to synchronize with the news ticker. Whether I wanted it or not. Early 2016. I was idling, as is my custom. I needed liquidity for the next London trip. I don't recall the specific logistics--likely, I just threw a question into the ether of Facebook: "Can I be of use to anyone?" A friend responded.

CHAPTER 28: THE DRILL AND THE VAMPIRE

In 2019, the government announced 'Drills to Disconnect the Russian Internet from the World.' They wanted to see if they could run the country on an intranet.  
My phone exploded. Foreign journalists asked: 'Will Russia go dark tomorrow?'  
I laughed. 'You can't unplug Russia,' I told them. 'Our internet wasn't built by the State; it was built by thousands of independent ISPs who laid cables in basements in the 90s. There is no main switch. It's a chaotic mesh of wires. To kill the internet, you'd have to bomb the sewers.'

Then, reality shifted into high-definition satire. I stepped into the role of Producer for *Empire V*.

Only in the middle of the day, when the production chaos reached its boiling point, would I step in. I would join the meetings, offer a few words of advice, and reset the temperature of the room.

I realized then: We are not just filming the Empire. We are the Empire. We were burning investor money to create an illusion, while high on the very Glamour we were supposed to be critiquing. CHAPTER 36: THE SILENT CITY AND THE NEW DEAL

CHAPTER 37: THE POISON AND THE RETURN

In February 2024, when they finally killed him and I stepped out into the freezing Moscow night with a can of spray paint, I made the exact same choice. CHAPTER 40: THE VODKA AND THE VOW

It is the only jurisdiction left where I possess absolute sovereignty. I am not an observer here; I am the Actor. In a world that is constantly trying to reduce you to an object, creating art is the ultimate way to remain a subject.

This wasn't just an emotional impulse; it was a synthesis. It was a handshake between the sincerity of the boy who just wants to draw and the experience of the analyst who knows how to capture a global market.  
I looked at the market and the trends, and then I stopped. For twenty years, I had traded in words. I sold explanations, logic, and "Digital Sovereignty" strategies. But look where logic got us. The analysts failed. The strategies burned. Words became radioactive. I realized I couldn't explain anything anymore. You cannot "analyze" Bucha. You cannot write a "strategy" for grief. I picked up the marker not because it was a "product," but because I had run out of words.

I chose the Heart not as a logo, but as a white flag--a surrender to the fact that in a world of complex evil, the only counter-argument is simple, stupid, defenseless love. It wasn't an "efficient interface." It was the only language I had left that hadn't been poisoned by the state.

I don't have illusions. I have a cynical, x-ray vision of the world. I know how the machine works, how to build a brand, and how to structure a deal. My forty years of "silence" weren't wasted. I wasn't suffering; I was arming myself. I came back to the canvas not as a child, but as a predator with a paintbrush.  
And I had one more secret weapon: Love. It sounds sentimental, but it is a structural fact. I grew up in a fortress of love. My family--despite the Empire collapsing and the madness of the 90s--always held that perimeter. I was loved as a child, and I am loved now. This is my fuel. It gives me a confidence that no amount of money can buy.

So, what to draw? I combined the two things I had: the Love (my fuel) and the Cynicism (my tool). I chose the Heart. I approached it not as a "creative flow," but as a professional challenge. I made a public pledge: In the year 2022, I will draw 2,022 unique hearts.  
It was a contract with myself. It was discipline. It was a daily prayer performed with a stylus. I didn't know yet that I was preparing my soul for the year when the world would run out of love completely. I was building a stockpile of ammunition for a war I didn't know was coming.

It sounds like a sudden epiphany--the blind boy regaining his sight--but let's be honest: that chronology is just a requirement of dramaturgy. A narrative needs a clean break, but reality is usually a gradient.  
Of course, I had been experimenting for years sporadically. Sketching wasn't a calling then; it was a utility. It was a functional skill to explain a UI interface or prototype an idea on a napkin when words failed. It wasn't academic art; it was visual shorthand.

My first "serious" artifact materialized in late 2019. It was a birthday gift for Arsen Revazov--a polymath: photographer, writer, entrepreneur, and an old friend of Anton Nossik. What do you give a man who has everything? Shortly before this, Arsen had held a solo exhibition at the Tretyakov Gallery. The context provided the concept. I took a canvas, one meter by one meter, and painted a massive inscription: "Arsen Revazov. Oil on Canvas." It was literal. I used actual black and white oil paints. It took forever to dry. It was meta-irony, physicalized. I hope he liked it.

There were other probes. I toyed with photogrammetry--poor man's 3D scanning. Naturally, I considered leveraging the family franchise: death masks. It would have been a stylish, digital continuation of my Great-Grandfather's work, but it remained a hypothesis. I played with conceptualism--trying to render Marshall McLuhan's "The Medium is the Message" across three different physical mediums simultaneously.  
I was playing. But there is a key distinction: in all these experiments, I never crossed the line into self-identification. I wasn't an "Artist." I was a tourist. And more importantly, there was no conversion. No market. No sale. Unlike the Hearts--that was where the game became real.

What did "being an artist" mean in a practical sense? It was too late for academic training, and I didn't have the time. I started working any way I could. I posted my results online and tried to sell them. Often, it worked. Some buyers were friends who probably wanted to support my initiative, but others were total strangers. I like to believe that regardless of the technical execution, I managed to convey a sense of warmth even then.

I spent forty years jumping between industries, looking for the right container. I finally found it in the Art Market and the Canvas. For the first time, I am certain: I will not quit this.  
The 'Ordinary World' operates on a strict script: pick a lane, suffer in it for thirty years, and receive a gold watch. Deviating from this is 'considered' a failure. But that phrase--'it is considered'--is the ultimate trap. Why exactly should I suffer just because some invisible committee decided what is correct?

However, there is a crucial footnote to my lifestyle: I am not a missionary. I do not walk into your house and demand you live like me. I don't care how you live. I don't want disciples; I want distance. I found my frequency. I simply ask that you do not jam the signal.

Beyond the obsession with spending all my money on materials--trying everything from watercolor to acrylic--I realized the main secret of any profession: the understanding that you will do it regardless of the outcome. I knew I wouldn't quit.  
Sales and success are often situational; they don't always depend on the amount of effort. My past confirmed this. I became a successful photographer without experience just by catching a wave. I achieved things on the internet by hitting a trend at the right time. But this--this was a long road without clear prospects.

It took me forty years. Forty years of wearing masks, playing roles, and building careers I didn't want.  
People ask: "Isn't it too late?" or "Wasn't it a waste?"  
No. Finding your own coordinates is the most expensive project you will ever undertake. The bill is astronomical. I paid for it with years of stress, with health, with money, with stability.  
But the ROI (Return on Investment) is infinite. The moment you stop pretending and start being--whether you are drawing hearts or sweeping streets--the friction disappears. The engine stops overheating. You are finally driving your own car. It is expensive, yes. But it is the only asset that cannot be devalued by the Central Bank.

I expanded the universe to include the Angel and the Devil, but don't look for theology here. I designed them to be frictionless. They have no gender, no religion, and no fixed morality. They are non-binary avatars of mood; sometimes the Angel is a bastard, sometimes the Devil is a savior.  
My goal was to create a symbol stack that requires zero context. I wanted a product that a child in Tokyo and a banker in New York could understand instantly, without reading a manifesto or knowing my biography. It is the ultimate universal language: scalable, transferable, and absolute.

People often ask: why the heart?  
To begin with, it is a tactical choice. If an artist paints a portrait or a landscape, the viewer feels qualified to judge it--to call it "bad" or "unskilled." But if you draw a heart, criticism hits a wall of silence. It is almost impossible to call a heart "bad." It simply is what it is. It is an unimpeachable form.  
Then there is the question of accessibility. Much of contemporary art demands a vast amount of institutional context. To truly "understand" a piece, one often needs two degrees just to decipher the artist's intent, the historical period, and the socio-political subtext. The heart requires none of this. It bypasses the intellect and moves directly to recognition.  
There is also something fundamentally human and unpretentious about it. I see this clearly in the winter, when the first snow covers the cars in Moscow or London, and children use their fingers to trace hearts on the windshields. You walk past, you see that simple shape, and you feel a momentary, uncomplicated joy.  
Perhaps this sounds overly pragmatic, but art, at its core, serves a decorative and functional purpose. While high-concept performances and profound political statements have their place in a specific moment, they rarely survive the transition to the private sphere. Globally, we want art we can live with--on our walls, in our gardens. The heart is the ultimate universal symbol in this regard. It is appropriate in a bedroom, a kitchen, or even on a grave. It is a symbol that fits the entirety of the human experience without the friction of explanation.

CHAPTER 41: THE TEETH AND THE TRAP

Back in Moscow, on the surface, life was booming. The Erotic Alphabet was selling. I was making plans for a Foundation. But the air was getting thin. You could feel the tension on your skin.

I met with my lawyer, Oleg Eliseev. We had first met years earlier, in 2017, when he got me out of a cell after my arrest under Article 228. Now, in 2022, we sat on a bench near the Novinsky Passage, watching expensive cars drive along the Garden Ring.  
Oleg wasn't just dealing with drug cases anymore. The times had changed, and the criminal code with them. He was defending Ivan Safronov, the journalist accused of treason. The charges were absurd--passing publicly available information to a Czech academic was labeled espionage. But absurdity was the new normal.  
"It's a lottery now, Anton," he said, lighting a cigarette. "They can come for anyone. Just like that. For nothing. The logic of the repression is that there is no logic."

The absurdity of the moment was perfectly captured by a wedding. My friend, a prominent lawyer, married Varvara Karaulova--the student who had been imprisoned for trying to join ISIS. It was the social event of the season in our circles: the Defender and the Prisoner, united in matrimony. It felt like a feast during a plague. We were celebrating, working, and launching projects, but we all checked the news feed with a sense of dread every morning. The noose was tightening. We just didn't know when it would snap.

I didn't know him personally, but I had met his wife once in London--a casual dinner, nothing significant. Just another connection in the tight web of the Moscow opposition. His lawyers, Maria Eismont and Vadim Prokhorov, were part of the same ecosystem. We all knew each other, loosely, the way people do in small circles that believe they're large.

On the other side, I tried to play the role of the digital freedom fighter one last time. I was working with Clostra, the same team behind FireChat. The product was NewNode--a protocol for data transfer designed to bypass censorship.  
But I realized something during the Kazakhstan coup in January 2022. I had been to Kazakhstan before--as an election observer in Astana back when Nursultan Nazarbayev still ruled. I knew the region. When the internet went dark during the protests, I understood the reality of the conflict. My job was to offer this tool to activists--to people willing to go onto the street and fight the riot police.

I felt a profound fatigue. I looked in the mirror. I was over 40. I was an adult. And I wanted to choose my battles. "War is not my genre," I admitted to myself. "I am not a partisan. I am an artist."  
The contract with Clostra ended. I took the money and stepped away from the barricades. I retreated to my sofa in the center of Moscow. "Where am I?" I thought. "And where is the reality?" The contrast was deafening. How can you worry about the shade of red in a digital drawing when people you know are being designated as "Foreign Agents" and enemies of the state?

I adopted a new philosophy: Self-preservation. There is me. There is my family. There is everyone else. "There is no God but the Heart, and the Little Angel is his Prophet." It wasn't cynicism. It was a mental air-raid shelter. I knew that the horror outside was just beginning. In Russia, the war hadn't even truly started yet.  
So I started working on my portfolio--the paperwork for the UK Global Talent Visa. I prepared my exit. I waited for the knock on the door, whispering the new Moscow mantra: *Inshallah*.

Let's be precise about why I stayed. It wasn't just the comfort, the money, or the dental work. Those are excuses for the poor. The real reason was much darker and infinitely more arrogant: I thought I had Admin Access.  
For twenty years, I had operated in the grey zone between the State and the Street. I drank whiskey with the men who wrote the laws and smoked joints with the men who broke them. I walked through walls. While others were getting stopped at the entrance, I was being waved through to the VIP room.  
I developed a false sense of physics. I believed that my network was a physical shield. "They won't touch me," I told myself. "I know too many people. I am too visible. I am part of the landscape." I looked at the repression as a weather phenomenon--something that gets the tourists wet, but spares the locals who know the shortcuts.  
This is the ultimate trap of the Moscow insider: the belief that you have a personal agreement with the Chaos. I thought I was smart enough to dance between the raindrops. I looked at the panic of the emigrants with a sneer. "Amateurs," I thought. "They run because they don't know how to negotiate."  
I wasn't staying because I was brave. I was staying because I was conceited. I truly believed that my biography, my surname, and my phone book gave me immunity.  
I forgot the most basic rule of the Russian system: There are no untouchables. There is only a queue. And standing in the VIP line doesn't mean you won't be executed; it just means you'll be executed in a more comfortable chair.

The confidence was reinforced by absurdity. One day, my phone rang. It was Radio Zvezda--the official station of the Ministry of Defense. The mouthpiece of the war.  
"Anton Pavlovich," a polite female voice said. "We would like your expert commentary on the digitalization of the economy."  
I paused. The country was bombing its neighbor. I was a known liberal, a "National Traitor" in their terminology. And the Ministry of Defense was calling me for a quote on IT trends.  
"Do you know who you are calling?" I asked.  
"Yes, of course," she replied, unfazed. "You are in our database as an expert."  
I refused, of course. But the call confirmed my theory: The System is not a predator; it is a broken robot. It doesn't know friend from foe. It just executes scripts. And as long as I was in the database as an "Expert," not an "Enemy," I thought I was safe.

I always had problems with my teeth. It wasn't the result of lifestyle or neglect; it was a genetic lottery. My jaw was a crumbling infrastructure project that I had been trying to patch up for decades. In my youth, I tried to fix it within the Soviet system. While I lay in the dentist's chair, the world prepared to burn.

My answer was always the same: "Matvey, I'm an artist now. I draw hearts. I don't care about geopolitics." The war was my ultimate failure as an analyst. It was a catastrophic glitch in my predictive model. I watched the dreary government meetings and dismissed them as theater. I thought it was a geriatric hallucination. I saw old men rambling about imperial grievances and assumed it was just noise. My logic was chronological: this is the 21st century--we have Tesla, Starlink, and AI. Meanwhile, they were discussing *lapti*--medieval bast shoes. I refused to believe anyone would try to drag a modern country back to the 16th century by force.

My second error was sociological. For years, the Russian opposition believed that the people were asleep only because the repression was targeted. We thought that to wake the masses, you needed a shock that touched everyone at once. We were wrong. Even the horror of war failed to penetrate the absolute apathy. I checked the local district chats and waited for the outrage. There was nothing. Zero reaction. The population didn't wake up; they just rolled over.

I was busy with something else: I was waiting for my veneers. I walked around Moscow with temporary plastic teeth, my mouth numb from anesthesia, preparing to flash a million-dollar smile at a world that was about to go dark. I lay there in the dark on Kutuzovsky Prospect, feeling the temporary plastic in my mouth. It was a monstrous prioritization of comfort over safety, a triumph of aesthetics over survival, but I refused to run away with unfinished infrastructure in my mouth. I stayed in Moscow. For three months, while the world screamed, I calmly visited my dentist. I lived in the eye of the storm, finalizing the porcelain veneers. By May, the job was done. I walked out with a permanent, blindingly white smile. Only then, with my affairs in order and my face reconstructed, did I buy a ticket. I woke up, brushed my temporary teeth (at first), then my expensive new ones, drew my daily Heart, and walked through Regent's Park. But my phone was a window into a nightmare. Then you have breakfast. Then you sit down to draw hearts because that is the only thing keeping you sane."

My Israeli Darkon was a travel document, not a residency permit. When my time was up, I had no "Global Talent" visa and no legal right to stay. The West opened the door only slightly, then politely asked me to leave. The second reason was more human: money. I am a hedonist. I am used to a specific quality of life--taxis, good wine, the ability to solve problems with a credit card. In Moscow, even during the war, I had "flow." I had status. I could live like a king in a burning castle. London is a cruel city for the poor. The prospect of being a forty-year-old refugee counting pennies in Tesco terrified me. I feared poverty more than I feared the abstract repression of the State. I traded safety for lifestyle. It was a mistake born of luxury, not ideology.

But beneath the logistics, there was a structural defect in my character: I have a pathological allergy to being pushed. Whether it's the State, the Opposition, or History itself--the moment an external force tries to dictate my path, I dig my heels in. When the crowd runs, my instinct is to stand still. Not out of bravery, but out of spite. I refused to let the geopolitical weather dictate my breakfast. I didn't want a life that was a reaction to Putin. I wanted to live my own life. Some chose to leave and wash floors in foreign countries for the sake of a clean conscience. I do not judge them, but that was not my path. I demanded the right to live within my own mental borders. I reserve the exclusive right to script my own biography, even if that script looks like a mistake to everyone else. I didn't return to Moscow because I was a patriot. I returned because I was a law-abiding tourist in the eyes of the British Home Office and a stubborn bastard in the eyes of history.

I arrived at my apartment to an empty fridge, months of dust, and the stale air of a life on pause. I walked out onto the street with my brand new, permanent, blindingly white Hollywood teeth. "Be careful," he warned. "The lottery is still running. And the odds are getting worse."  
I was a commuter between Heaven and Hell. I had to finish my dental work and close my affairs. So I stayed. I waited. And I drew.

CHAPTER 42: THE SILENCE AND THE COST

The Concentration Camp Effect.  
There are two questions constantly thrown at us from the outside: "Why don't they protest?" and "Why don't you leave?" They are two sides of the same coin, and they must be answered together.

The lack of resistance has already been dissected in tonnes of research. It's deep in the firmware. Tolstoy captured it in *War and Peace*--the peasant telling the master that they don't need this "freedom," they are simply used to their lot. Even in my childhood, in the late Soviet 80s, the "cultural layer"--the dissidents, the non-conformists--was a statistical error compared to the gray mass.

Add to this the genetic memory of repression. From kindergarten, you are taught: "Hush. Keep your head down. It's dangerous." Multiply that by state propaganda designed to sever horizontal ties, and the result is inevitable.  
It plays out like the old joke:  
"Do you love the Motherland?"  
"I do!"  
"Will you die for her?"  
"But if I die, who will be left to love her?"

In practice, this manifests as a total atrophy of collective action. One person steps into the square. The society watches. The person is arrested. The society rationalizes: "Why should I step in front of a bullet?" This isn't an accusation; it is a diagnosis. It is just how it happened.

The second question--"Why didn't you leave sooner?"--brings me back to my work as a consultant. It is the easiest job in the world. You walk into a company, tell them, "You are all idiots, here is what you need to do," take the envelope of cash, and leave without any responsibility for the outcome.

CHAPTER 45: THE RADIO AND THE CAROUSEL

I was taken to Detention Center No. 2. It wasn't a dungeon. It resembled a strict-regime summer camp. A cell for eight people. Bunk beds. The smell of cheap tobacco and unwashed men.

My cellmates were the usual debris of the city: drunk drivers, petty thieves, guys who didn't pay alimony. I was the only "Political." I introduced myself. Paradoxically, inside the perimeter, I was safe. The state had me; nothing worse could happen.

The Exchange Rate.  
It was the last honest marker left in the country. I used it as a remote diagnostic tool. Politics can lie, generals can lie, but the currency market is harder to rig. Every morning, I waited for the numbers. Dollar. Euro. As long as the Ruble wasn't crashing, I knew the apocalypse hadn't started yet.  
Day after day, the numbers held. Nothing was happening. The catastrophe was stable.

Life inside is a loop.  
**Food:** Edible. Office canteen level. I was squeamish, but I ate the salads.  
**Parcels:** Cigarettes (broken out of packs for safety), cookies, nuts, instant noodles. Everything poured into clear plastic bags.  
**Walks:** One hour a day in a tiny courtyard with a grate over the sky.  
**The staff:** Surprisingly human. My hack was politeness. They are used to scum. When you say "Hello," "Excuse me," "Please," the system crashes. They treat you like a human in return.

The guys smoked constantly. When we ran out of cigarettes at night, the guards would knock on the door: "Hey, guys, share some sugar and tea with Cell 4, they'll send you tobacco." Peaceful coexistence in hell.

But the fear was real. Not of the cell--you can survive a cell. The fear was the "Carousel."  
It was the new trend. You serve twelve days. At the exit, they arrest you again for fifteen days. And while you sit, they cook up the Big Case. Five to fifteen years.  
I had 288 hours to wait. I read. I read anything I could find to stop the internal monologue. Thinking in prison is dangerous.  
My lawyer visited once. It was enough to know that the machinery outside was still working. That someone was watching. That if I disappeared, people would notice.

Then, something went wrong.  
A new guy was brought in. An experienced convict by the look of him. The search must have been sloppy, because he managed to smuggle something in. He went to the toilet in the corner. Minutes later, the sound of choking.  
Overdose.  
We watched the body convulse. It was annoying. It interfered with sleep. We knocked on the door. "Call an ambulance."

They took him away. As a reward for our "good behavior"--for not letting a man die on their watch--they gave us a phone off-schedule to call our relatives.  
"I'm fine," I told the receiver. "Everything is fine."  
You can't say anything else. The walls have ears.

Halfway through the term, they hauled me to the Moscow City Court for an appeal. I was unwashed, smelling of the cell. The judge gave me ten minutes.  
"Appeal denied."  
Back to the cage. Just another stamp on the paperwork.

CHAPTER 46: THE HOODIE AND THE STAMP

The system made an error in my favor. My lawyer--Oleg Eliseev, the same man who had saved me in 2017--met me at the gates. When they released me, they returned my belongings. My phone. My wallet. My earrings.

The car started moving.
